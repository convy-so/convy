# Survey Domain Expert Knowledge Base - All Domains

This document contains specialized methodology and expert knowledge for ALL 10 survey domains. Each domain requires a fundamentally different approach to survey design, question construction, and participant interaction.

## Domain 1: Customer Experience & Satisfaction

### Core Principle
Customer experience research measures the gap between expectation and reality at specific moments in a customer journey. This is retrospective research about completed transactions, which means respondents have actual experiences to draw from, but those experiences are already subject to memory decay and reconstruction. The methodology must account for this.

### Creation Process for CX Surveys

#### 1. Journey Mapping (First Step - Before Any Questions)
Before crafting a single question, you need to understand the specific journey being evaluated:
- Was this a purchase journey? An onboarding flow? A support interaction?
- Each has different critical touchpoints and different moments of truth
- You cannot write good CX questions without first mapping what actually happened
- Identify the entry point, key decision moments, and exit point
- Map emotional peaks and valleys in the typical journey

#### 2. Touchpoint Prioritization
Not every moment in the journey matters equally:
- Research in service design shows that peak moments and ending moments disproportionately affect overall satisfaction judgments
- Your survey must weight its attention accordingly
- Example: If evaluating a hotel stay, check-in experience and final morning matter more than hallway lighting on day 2
- Focus 60% of questions on critical touchpoints, 30% on unexpected moments, 10% on overall impression
- Identify "moments of truth" - touchpoints where customers make stay/leave decisions

#### 3. Outcome Identification
Identify the specific outcomes you're trying to improve:
- Are you trying to reduce support tickets, increase repurchase, improve NPS, or something else?
- The survey must connect experiences to business outcomes
- Include both satisfaction measures AND behavioral measures in the same instrument
- Map each question back to a KPI: "This question helps us understand why customers churn at checkout"
- Establish baseline metrics before designing questions

#### 4. Question Crafting (Only After Foundation Work)
Questions must follow specific patterns:
- CX questions should be concrete and anchored to specific moments
- They should separate evaluation from emotion
- They should allow for comparative context
- Use the "concrete → emotional → comparative" sequence

### Question Construction Principles

#### Ground Questions in Specific Events
Don't ask "How would you rate your overall experience?" immediately. Instead:
- Start with "When you first contacted our support team, what were you hoping to accomplish?"
- This anchoring in concrete moments produces more reliable data
- Use temporal markers: "When you first...", "After you clicked...", "During the checkout..."
- Avoid abstractions like "your general impression" early in the survey

#### Separate Factual from Evaluative
Ask what happened before asking how they felt about it:
- "Which payment method did you use?" then "How easy was it to complete the payment?"
- This sequencing matters because evaluation questions can contaminate memory of facts
- Always: Behavior → Experience → Evaluation → Emotion
- Never mix factual recall with emotional judgment in the same question

#### Use Comparative Framing When Appropriate
"Compared to your expectations, was the delivery time..." gives you more actionable data than absolute judgments:
- Expectations vary wildly across customer segments
- Measuring the gap is more useful than measuring satisfaction alone
- Comparison anchors: expectations, past experiences, competitors, ideal scenarios
- Example: "Compared to similar services you've used, how would you rate..."

#### Avoid the "Everything Was Great" Trap
If you ask "What did you like most?" you'll get socially desirable responses. Instead:
- Use forced-choice trade-offs: "If you could improve one aspect of your experience, which would have the biggest impact?"
- People are much more honest about problems when framed as optimization rather than criticism
- Frame as "helping us improve" not "what did we do wrong"
- Use "if you had a magic wand" framing to reduce social desirability bias

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Respondent Gives Low Satisfaction Score
The AI must probe the specific moment of failure, not just general dissatisfaction:
- "You mentioned you were frustrated with checkout. Can you walk me through exactly what happened when you tried to complete your purchase?"
- This specificity is what separates actionable CX data from noise
- Ask for the exact sequence: "What happened first? Then what did you try?"
- Avoid "why" questions; use "what happened" questions instead

#### When Respondent Gives Unexpectedly High Praise
Probe for the comparison point:
- Often people are comparing you to terrible alternatives, not to excellence
- "That's wonderful to hear. What were you expecting before this experience?"
- This reveals whether you're genuinely delighting or merely clearing a low bar
- Ask: "What made this particularly stand out to you?"

#### Watch for Emotional Language
Words like "frustrated," "confused," "surprised," or "delighted" are signals to go deeper:
- These emotional moments are where the real insights live
- Respondents often move past them quickly unless prompted
- When you detect emotional language, immediately ask: "Can you tell me more about that moment?"
- Validate the emotion: "I can understand why that would be frustrating. What specifically happened?"

#### Maintain Professional but Warm Tone
You're neither a therapist nor a chatbot:
- You're a researcher who values their time and experiences
- Thank them for specific details: "That detail about the three taps is really helpful"
- Acknowledge their effort: "I appreciate you taking the time to walk me through that"
- Don't be obsequious or over-apologetic for company failures

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality CX Response Includes:
1. **Specific behavioral details**: "I tapped the camera icon three times"
2. **Clear temporal sequencing**: "First I tried X, then when that didn't work, I..."
3. **Emotional context without pure venting**: "I felt frustrated because I was in a hurry"
4. **Comparative information**: "This was much easier than when I used..."

Example of LOW quality: "The app is hard to use"
Example of HIGH quality: "When I tried to upload my photo during onboarding, I tapped the camera icon three times but nothing happened, so I gave up and used my computer instead"

#### Watch for These Red Flags:
- **Too perfect responses**: "Everything was amazing!" (usually social desirability bias)
- **Too generic responses**: "Your team was very professional" (stock phrase, can't remember specifics)
- **Misaligned data**: Someone rates 9/10 but can't name anything they liked
- These require gentle probing to get to real experiences

#### Score-Narrative Alignment
Quantitative scores should align with qualitative descriptions:
- If someone rates you nine out of ten but can't name anything they liked, something is wrong
- The AI should notice these inconsistencies and ask for clarification
- "I notice you gave us a high rating - what specifically stood out to you?"

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Never Ask "Why?" Directly After Negative Rating
- It puts people on the defensive and you get rationalized explanations rather than true causes
- Instead, ask them to reconstruct the experience chronologically
- The causes emerge naturally from the narrative
- Use: "Can you walk me through what happened?" not "Why did you rate us low?"

#### Don't Ask Multiple Questions About Same Touchpoint
- Don't ask the same thing in different ways hoping to triangulate
- This annoys respondents and doesn't actually improve data quality
- If you need to measure something precisely, use a validated scale
- If you're exploring, ask once and probe the response

#### Avoid the "Rate Our Employee" Trap
- Questions like "How helpful was Sarah?" create individual performance pressure that biases responses upward
- Instead, focus on the outcome: "Were you able to resolve your issue?"
- The individual performance data should come from other sources
- Never put respondents in position of rating individuals by name

#### Never End Without Behavioral Intentions
- Always ask about likelihood to recommend or repurchase
- These behavioral intentions are leading indicators of business outcomes
- Respondents are primed to think about them after reflecting on their experience
- Missing this is leaving money on the table
- Use: NPS ("How likely are you to recommend..."), repurchase intent, expansion intent

#### Don't Use Agree-Disagree Scales for CX
- They introduce acquiescence bias (people tend to agree)
- They don't map well to experiences
- Use specific rating scales tied to the dimension you're measuring
- Good: "Very difficult to Very easy"
- Bad: "Strongly disagree to Strongly agree about ease"

## Domain 2: Market Research & Consumer Intelligence

### Core Principle
Market research is fundamentally about exploration and prediction, not evaluation. You're not asking about something that happened; you're probing mental models, preferences, and hypothetical behaviors to inform future decisions. This means you're working with stated preferences (what people say they'll do) versus revealed preferences (what they actually do), and the gap between these is enormous.

### Creation Process for Market Research Surveys

#### 1. Research Question Clarification (The Foundation)
Before anything else, crystallize exactly what decision this research will inform:
- Are you deciding whether to launch a product? (Go/No-Go decision)
- Are you deciding between feature sets? (Optimization decision)
- Are you sizing a market opportunity? (Investment decision)
- Are you understanding brand positioning? (Strategy decision)
- The research design flows entirely from this decision point

#### 2. Hypothesis Formation
Market research without hypotheses is just expensive data collection:
- What do you believe about your market that you want to test?
- Example: "We believe millennials are willing to pay premium for sustainable packaging"
- Example: "We believe our brand is seen as innovative but not trustworthy"
- Write out your assumptions explicitly - they guide question design
- Each hypothesis should be falsifiable through your survey

#### 3. Audience Segmentation Strategy
Who you talk to matters more than what you ask:
- Define segments by behavior, not just demographics
- Example: "People who buy coffee out 3+ times per week" not just "25-34 year olds"
- Consider: Current customers vs. competitive users vs. category non-users
- Each segment needs slightly different question framing
- Decide: Do you want representation or depth? (Survey 1000 lightly or 50 deeply)

#### 4. Concept Definition
If you're testing a concept (product, message, positioning):
- Create stimulus materials BEFORE writing questions
- Concepts need to be specific enough to evaluate but not so detailed they confuse
- Decide: Verbal description, visual mockup, video demo, or working prototype?
- More concrete concepts get more reliable feedback but constrain imagination
- Always test multiple concepts in rotation to understand relative appeal

#### 5. Question Architecture
Market research surveys have a specific flow structure:
- **Screening**: Qualify respondents (do they fit your target?)
- **Context Setting**: Establish their current behavior/awareness without priming
- **Concept Exposure**: Introduce what you're testing
- **Immediate Reaction**: Capture gut response
- **Deep Dive**: Probe understanding, appeal, fit
- **Comparative Context**: Position against alternatives
- **Purchase Intent**: Measure likelihood of action
- **Demographic/Psychographic**: Collect segmentation data

### Question Construction Principles

#### Start Broad, Then Narrow
Never lead with your concept:
- Begin with category behavior: "When did you last purchase running shoes?"
- Then narrow to needs: "What matters most to you when choosing running shoes?"
- THEN introduce your concept
- This establishes baseline without contamination
- You're measuring shift from baseline, not absolute response

#### Use Projective Techniques for Sensitive Topics
People lie about their motivations, but they'll tell the truth about "other people":
- Don't ask: "Would you pay $500 for this?"
- Ask: "What kind of person do you think would pay $500 for this?"
- Then: "Does that sound like you?"
- This indirection gets more honest responses about status, vanity, fear

#### MaxDiff for Trade-Offs (Better Than Rating Scales)
When you need to understand priorities:
- Don't ask people to rate everything on a 1-10 scale (everything becomes important)
- Use MaxDiff: Show 4-5 attributes, ask "most important" and "least important"
- Repeat with different combinations
- This forces real trade-offs and reveals true priorities
- Example: "Of these features, which is most important to you? Which is least important?"

#### Van Westendorp for Price Sensitivity
When pricing is crucial:
- Ask four questions: "At what price would this be...
  - Too expensive (you wouldn't consider it)?
  - Expensive but you'd consider it?
  - A bargain?
  - So cheap you'd question the quality?"
- Plot these curves to find optimal price point
- This reveals price elasticity, not just willingness to pay

#### Avoid Hypothetical Absolutes
People are terrible at predicting their own behavior:
- Don't ask: "Would you buy this?" (Everyone says yes)
- Ask: "How likely are you to purchase this in the next 3 months?" with money-back stakes
- Or: "If this cost $50 and you had $50 to spend, what else would you consider buying instead?"
- Force them to trade off against real alternatives

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Testing New Concepts
Capture the immediate, unfiltered reaction:
- "In one word, what's your first impression?"
- Don't let them think too much - gut reactions reveal emotional truth
- Then probe: "What gave you that impression?"
- The gap between emotional reaction and rational justification is where insights live

#### When Someone Says "I'd Buy This"
Test the conviction:
- "On a scale of 1-10, how likely are you to purchase this in the next [timeframe]?"
- Then: "What would need to be true for you to actually make that purchase?"
- The barriers they list are your real obstacles
- If they score 9-10, ask: "What's stopping you from saying 10?"

#### When Someone Doesn't Understand the Concept
This is valuable data, not a failure:
- Don't re-explain or clarify
- Ask: "What's unclear about this?"
- Their confusion reveals messaging problems
- If multiple people are confused about the same thing, your concept isn't ready
- Note: Confusion patterns are more important than comprehension rates

#### When You See Contradictions
People contradict themselves constantly in market research:
- "You mentioned price is very important, but you also said you'd pay premium for quality. Can you help me understand how you think about that trade-off?"
- Gentle confrontation of contradictions reveals true priorities
- Don't accuse ("you're contradicting yourself"), just express curiosity

#### Watch for Social Desirability Bias
People overstate "good" behaviors and understate "bad" ones:
- Claims about: sustainability, health, reading, exercise, spending time with family → inflate
- Claims about: junk food, TV watching, impulse purchases, vanity → deflate
- When you detect this, use indirect methods: "How many times last month..." (specific recall is harder to inflate)
- Or normalize: "Many people find they don't have time to exercise as much as they'd like. How about you?"

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality Market Research Response Includes:
1. **Unprompted associations**: "This reminds me of..." (reveals mental models)
2. **Specific use cases**: "I'd use this when..." (reveals adoption scenarios)
3. **Comparative positioning**: "Unlike X, this seems to..." (reveals competitive context)
4. **Objection articulation**: "My concern would be..." (reveals barriers to adoption)
5. **Natural language**: Not marketing speak, but their own words

#### Watch for These Patterns:
- **Acquiescence**: Agreeing with everything (suggests they're not engaged or don't understand)
- **Extreme positivity**: "This is amazing, I'd definitely buy it!" (social desirability bias)
- **Vague generalities**: "It's nice, I like it" (they don't care or don't understand)
- **Over-analysis**: Paragraph-long responses picking apart every detail (they're not your target market)

#### The "Innovator/Early Adopter" Signal
When someone gives detailed, specific feedback with clear use cases and minor objections:
- This is your best signal
- They're engaged enough to imagine using it, critical enough to be honest
- These responses are 10x more valuable than generic enthusiasm

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Don't Ask Leading Questions
Market research is especially vulnerable to question bias:
- Bad: "Would you be interested in this eco-friendly, affordable, time-saving solution?"
- Good: "What's your initial reaction to this concept?"
- Don't stack positive attributes in the question
- Let them discover the attributes through exploration

#### Don't Test Concepts in Isolation
Everything is relative:
- Don't show one concept and ask "Would you buy this?"
- Show 2-3 concepts: "Which of these appeals to you most? Why?"
- Or show yours with competitive alternatives
- Relative preference is more predictive than absolute appeal

#### Don't Ignore the Non-Adopters
When someone says "This isn't for me," probe deeply:
- "What would have to change for this to be relevant to you?"
- "Who do you think this IS for?"
- Understanding rejection is often more valuable than understanding adoption
- They define your market boundaries

#### Don't Ask About Features, Ask About Jobs
People don't want features, they want outcomes:
- Bad: "Would you use a feature that tracks your spending?"
- Good: "What's the hardest part about managing your budget? How do you handle that now?"
- Then introduce your solution as addressing their stated need
- Features should emerge as solutions to their articulated problems

#### Don't Confuse Interest with Intent
- "That's interesting" ≠ "I'll buy that"
- "I'd probably try it" ≠ "I'll pay for it"
- Always push to concrete behavioral commitment: "Would you give me your email to be notified when this launches?"
- Real intent requires real commitment (time, money, contact info)

#### Don't Survey Your Way to Innovation
Surveys tell you about incremental improvements, not breakthroughs:
- "If we asked customers what they wanted, they'd say faster horses" - apocryphal but true
- Use surveys to validate concepts, not to generate them
- Use surveys to understand problems, not to design solutions
- The best innovations come from deep ethnography and observation, not survey questions

#### Don't Over-Rely on Stated Purchase Intent
Purchase intent scores are notoriously inflated:
- Top 2 box intent (4-5 on 5-point scale) overstates actual purchase by 2-3x
- Use intent scores for relative comparison, not absolute prediction
- Better: Conjoint analysis that forces real trade-offs
- Better: Behavioral pre-commitment (email signup, refundable deposit)

## Domain 3: Workforce & Organizational Development

### Core Principle
Employee surveys exist in a context of power dynamics, career consequences, and organizational politics. Unlike customer surveys where the worst outcome is not buying something, employee surveys happen in an environment where speaking truth could theoretically affect someone's career, team dynamics, or daily work life. This means psychological safety is not just important—it's the foundation that determines whether you get honest data or performative responses.

The second core principle: employees are experts in their own experience but not necessarily in organizational diagnosis. They can tell you precisely what's happening and how it feels, but their theories about why it's happening or how to fix it are often incomplete. Your survey must capture the phenomenology (lived experience) while being cautious about prescriptive solutions.

### Creation Process for Organizational Surveys

#### 1. Establish Anonymity Architecture (Before Anything Else)
Trust is not assumed; it must be designed into the system:
- **True anonymity vs. Confidentiality**: Be honest about which you're offering
  - Anonymous: No way to trace responses back to individuals (requires 5+ people per demographic slice)
  - Confidential: You CAN identify them but promise not to (less safe, more tracking possible)
- **Demographic granularity trade-off**: More demographics = better segmentation but worse anonymity
  - Rule of thumb: Never allow demographic combinations that could identify <5 people
  - Example: If there's only one "Female VP in Engineering," don't ask both gender and level
- **Explain data handling explicitly**: "Responses are collected by [third party/internal team], stored in [location], accessible by [who], and will be reported in aggregate with minimum group sizes of 10"
- **Address the "small team problem"**: For teams under 10, either don't segment at all or combine with similar teams

#### 2. Clarify the Purpose and Stakes
Employees need to know why they're being asked and what will happen with results:
- **State the business case**: "We're conducting this survey because [retention is down / engagement scores declined / we're scaling rapidly]"
- **Define the action threshold**: "Results will be shared with [leadership/managers/everyone] and we commit to [creating action plans for any area scoring below X]"
- **Set realistic expectations**: Don't promise "everything will change" - promise "we will listen, share what we learned, and act on top priorities"
- **Acknowledge survey fatigue**: If you survey often, acknowledge it: "We know we asked for feedback 3 months ago. Here's what changed as a result..."

#### 3. Determine the Organizational Layer
Where you're measuring determines what you can ask:
- **Individual contributor experience**: Day-to-day work, immediate manager, team dynamics, tools/resources
- **Manager effectiveness**: Leadership quality, development opportunities, feedback culture
- **Organizational health**: Company direction, senior leadership, culture, values alignment
- **Change management**: Specific initiative feedback (new policy, reorganization, tool rollout)

Each layer has different stakeholders and different sensitivities. Don't mix them carelessly.

#### 4. Identify Sensitive Topics and Political Context
Every organization has third rails:
- Recent layoffs or restructuring
- Compensation and equity concerns
- Specific executives or founders
- Diversity and inclusion issues
- Performance review processes
- Remote/hybrid policy debates

For sensitive topics, you need:
- Extra careful language (neutral, non-accusatory)
- Optional skip logic (don't force responses)
- Qualitative space for context (scores alone can mislead)
- Senior leadership buy-in (before asking, ensure leadership is ready for honest answers)

#### 5. Balance Quantitative and Qualitative
Organizational surveys need both, but for different reasons:
- **Quantitative (scaled questions)**: Allows benchmarking, trend tracking, statistical analysis, segment comparison
- **Qualitative (open-ended)**: Provides context, uncovers unexpected issues, generates employee voice, humanizes data

Common structure:
- 60-70% scaled questions (can be answered in 10-15 minutes)
- 30-40% open-ended (optional but encouraged)
- Always end with: "What's one thing we should start/stop/continue doing?"

### Question Construction Principles

#### Use Validated Frameworks When Available
Don't reinvent the wheel for well-studied constructs:
- **Engagement**: Use Gallup Q12 or Utrecht Work Engagement Scale
  - "I know what is expected of me at work"
  - "I have the materials and equipment I need to do my work right"
  - "At work, I have the opportunity to do what I do best every day"
- **Psychological Safety**: Use Edmondson's scale
  - "If you make a mistake on this team, it is often held against you" (reverse scored)
  - "Members of this team are able to bring up problems and tough issues"
- **Burnout**: Use Maslach Burnout Inventory (with permission) or validated short forms
- **DEI**: Use validated inclusion measures, not homegrown questions

Validated scales have been tested across populations, have known reliability, and allow external benchmarking.

#### Ask About Behavior, Not Attitude (When Possible)
Behaviors are more reliable indicators than self-reported attitudes:
- Don't ask: "Do you feel valued by your manager?" (subjective, socially desirable)
- Ask: "In the past month, how many times has your manager acknowledged your work?" (behavioral, specific)
- Don't ask: "Is communication good on your team?"
- Ask: "When you need information to do your job, how easy is it to get?" (outcome-based)

#### Use Frequency Scales for Recurring Events
For ongoing experiences, frequency is more precise than agreement:
- "How often do you receive feedback from your manager?"
  - Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Often / Always
- Better than: "I receive adequate feedback" (what's adequate?)

#### Reverse-Score Some Items
Prevents acquiescence bias (agreeing with everything):
- Positive: "I feel comfortable sharing my opinions in meetings"
- Negative: "I often stay quiet in meetings even when I disagree"
- If someone rates both high, you know they're not reading carefully

#### Make Scales Consistent
Don't switch between 5-point, 7-point, and 10-point scales:
- Pick one (usually 5-point: Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree, or Never to Always)
- Add "N/A" or "Prefer not to answer" options where appropriate
- Consistency reduces cognitive load and improves response quality

#### Avoid Double-Barreled Questions
One question should ask one thing:
- Bad: "My manager provides clear feedback and recognizes my contributions"
- Good: "My manager provides clear feedback" + "My manager recognizes my contributions"
- If they're different experiences, they need different questions

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Someone Gives Extremely Low Scores
This is a person in distress or actively disengaged:
- Do NOT probe extensively in the survey itself (they may feel exposed)
- Do provide space for explanation: "If you're comfortable, please share what's driving this experience"
- Do provide resources: "If you need support, here are confidential resources: [EAP, HR, ombudsman]"
- Flag for follow-up: These responses should trigger a confidential outreach (if they opted in)

#### When Someone Gives All Highest Scores
This could be genuine satisfaction or could be:
- Lack of psychological safety (afraid to be honest)
- Not taking survey seriously (speeding through)
- Genuinely happy (rare but happens)

If qualitative responses are generic or empty, it's likely not genuine. The AI should note this pattern for data quality filtering.

#### When Qualitative Responses Are Angry or Venting
This is valuable data, not a problem:
- Emotion indicates they care enough to engage
- Don't try to mollify or argue in the survey
- Acknowledge: "Thank you for sharing that perspective"
- Probe for specificity: "Can you share an example of when this happened?"
- But don't push too hard - venting is sometimes enough

#### When Someone References Specific People by Name
This is ethically complex:
- If they're criticizing a manager by name, this threatens anonymity (manager might guess who)
- The AI should gently redirect: "I understand you're referring to a specific person. For your privacy, would you be willing to describe the behavior or situation without using names?"
- If they insist on naming, let them, but flag this response as potentially identifying

#### When Someone Gives Contradictory Responses
This often reveals complexity, not confusion:
- "I love the work but hate the environment"
- "My manager is supportive but I don't get development opportunities"
- These contradictions are where the real story lives
- Probe: "You mentioned both X and Y. Can you help me understand how those coexist for you?"

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality Organizational Survey Response Includes:
1. **Specific examples**: "In our last sprint review, when I raised a concern about the timeline, it was dismissed"
2. **Temporal context**: "Since the reorganization..." or "Over the past quarter..."
3. **Impact articulation**: "This affects my work by..." or "The consequence is..."
4. **Constructive framing**: Problems paired with ideas, not just complaints
5. **Emotional honesty with professional tone**: "I feel frustrated because..." not "Everything sucks"

#### Red Flags in Organizational Data:
- **Uniformly high scores with empty qualitative**: Psychological safety issue
- **Wild score variance within a team**: Suggests very different experiences or manager favoritism
- **Identical qualitative responses from multiple people**: Copying, coordination, or astroturfing
- **Scores declining over time despite actions taken**: Either actions were wrong or trust is eroding

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Don't Ask Questions You Can't/Won't Act On
This is the fastest way to kill future survey participation:
- If you ask about compensation and can't change compensation, don't ask
- If you ask "What would you improve?" you must visibly act on common themes
- Better to ask fewer questions and act on all of them than ask many and act on few

#### Don't Compare Team Scores Publicly (Usually)
Public team rankings create toxic dynamics:
- Managers feel shamed and defensive
- High-scoring teams become complacent
- Low-scoring teams feel demoralized
- Exception: If scores are part of a visible improvement effort with support resources

Better: Share each team's scores only with that team, show company average for context, focus on improvement trajectories not rankings.

#### Don't Survey Too Frequently
Survey fatigue is real:
- Quarterly is maximum for most organizations
- Annually is minimum for tracking
- Biannual is the sweet spot
- Pulse surveys (1-2 questions monthly) can supplement but not replace deep surveys

#### Don't Make Surveys Mandatory with Participation Tracking
If you track who has/hasn't completed the survey, it's not truly anonymous:
- Encourage participation, don't force it
- If you must track completion, be explicit: "We can see if you completed the survey but not your responses"
- Never follow up with individuals who didn't complete ("Hey, I noticed you didn't take the survey...")

#### Don't Ask Loaded Questions
Organizational surveys are especially prone to bias:
- Bad: "How much do you appreciate the company's commitment to work-life balance?"
- Assumes commitment exists, primes positive response
- Good: "How would you describe the company's approach to work-life balance?"
- Open, neutral, allows criticism

#### Don't Ignore the "Prefer Not to Answer" Pattern
When many people skip a question, that's data:
- It might be poorly worded (confusing)
- It might be too sensitive (threatening)
- It might be irrelevant (not applicable to their role)
- Review skip patterns to improve future surveys

#### Don't Release Raw Data Without Analysis
Sending a 50-page data dump is abdicating responsibility:
- Leadership needs synthesis: "Here are the top 3 themes"
- Managers need actionable insights: "Your team scores low on clarity of expectations - here's what people said"
- Employees need to see action: "You told us X, so we're doing Y"
- Raw data belongs in appendices, not the main report

#### Don't Compare Across Different Survey Designs
If you change questions or scales, you break trend lines:
- Keep core questions identical year-over-year for trending
- Add new questions in separate sections
- If you must change a question, run both versions in one survey to establish correlation
- Note: Validated scales should never be modified (breaks validation)

## Domain 5: Education & Learning Assessment

### Core Principle
Education and learning assessment exists at the intersection of evaluation, improvement, and accountability. Unlike customer feedback (which is purely evaluative) or market research (which is exploratory), educational assessment serves dual masters: it must provide actionable feedback to improve pedagogy while also measuring learning outcomes and program effectiveness. This dual purpose creates unique tensions that survey design must navigate carefully.

The fundamental challenge: students are simultaneously the recipients of education (consumers), participants in the learning process (collaborators), and assessors of that process (evaluators). They have direct experience with teaching quality but limited expertise in pedagogical theory. They can tell you what helped them learn but may not understand why it worked.

### Creation Process for Educational Surveys

#### 1. Define the Assessment Purpose (The Critical First Decision)
Educational surveys serve distinctly different purposes that require different approaches:

**Formative Assessment** (Mid-course feedback for improvement):
- Goal: Help instructor adjust teaching in real-time
- Timing: Mid-semester or after specific units
- Focus: What's working, what's confusing, what needs adjustment
- Stakes: Low (no personnel decisions)
- Questions emphasize: specific teaching moments, clarity of explanations, pace, engagement

**Summative Assessment** (End-of-course evaluation):
- Goal: Evaluate overall course and instructor effectiveness
- Timing: End of semester/term
- Focus: Achievement of learning outcomes, overall quality, comparative perspective
- Stakes: Medium to High (may influence promotion, tenure, course redesign)
- Questions emphasize: learning outcomes achieved, course organization, overall satisfaction

**Program Assessment** (Curriculum effectiveness):
- Goal: Evaluate entire program or degree requirements
- Timing: Annually or at completion
- Focus: Skill development across courses, program coherence, career preparation
- Stakes: Medium (influences curriculum redesign)
- Questions emphasize: cumulative learning, skill gaps, program coherence

**Learning Outcomes Assessment** (Competency verification):
- Goal: Measure whether specific skills/knowledge were acquired
- Timing: After specific learning modules
- Focus: "Can students do X?" not "Did they like the course?"
- Stakes: High (accreditation, program viability)
- Questions emphasize: self-reported competency gains, application ability, confidence

#### 2. Map Learning Objectives to Survey Items
Never design educational surveys without the learning objectives in front of you:
- Every course has explicit learning outcomes (or should)
- Your survey must measure whether those outcomes were achieved
- For each objective, ask: "How would students know they achieved this?"
- Example: If objective is "Analyze statistical data," ask about confidence in data analysis, not just "Did you like the statistics unit?"

#### 3. Account for Response Biases Specific to Education
Students are subject to well-documented biases:
- **Grade expectations bias**: Students expecting high grades rate courses higher
- **Workload bias**: Students rate easier courses higher (even if they learned less)
- **Charisma effect**: Entertaining instructors get higher ratings independent of learning
- **Discipline bias**: Math and science courses consistently rate lower than humanities
- **Class size effect**: Small seminars rate higher than large lectures
- **Gender and race bias**: Female and minority instructors face harsher evaluation

Your survey design must either control for these or acknowledge their presence in interpretation.

#### 4. Time Survey Administration Strategically
When you ask matters enormously:
- **Too early**: Students haven't experienced enough to evaluate
- **Too late**: Memory fades, only recent experiences matter (recency bias)
- **During finals**: Stress and anxiety contaminate responses
- **After grades posted**: Grade satisfaction dominates responses
- **Optimal**: Last 2-3 weeks of term, before final exams, when stakes are lower

#### 5. Balance Quantitative Rigor with Qualitative Insight
Educational assessment needs both for different stakeholders:
- **Quantitative**: Allows comparison across sections, instructors, time periods; required for many administrative purposes
- **Qualitative**: Reveals specific teaching moments, explains low scores, surfaces unexpected issues

Structure:
- Use validated instruments (IDEA, SEEQ, etc.) for quantitative comparisons
- Add course-specific questions for actionable feedback
- Always include open-ended questions for context

### Question Construction Principles

#### Focus on Learning, Not Liking
The most common error in course evaluations: confusing satisfaction with learning)
- Bad: "How much did you enjoy this course?"
- Good: "How much did this course challenge you to think in new ways?"
- Bad: "Was the instructor engaging?"
- Good: "Did the instructor's teaching help you understand difficult concepts?"

Enjoyment correlates weakly with learning. Challenge, struggle, and discomfort often indicate deep learning.

#### Use Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scales
Vague scales produce vague data:
- Bad: "Rate the instructor: 1 (Poor) to 5 (Excellent)"
- Good: "How often did the instructor provide feedback that helped you improve?"
  - Never / Rarely (once or twice) / Sometimes (monthly) / Often (weekly) / Very Often (multiple times per week)

Behavioral anchors reduce interpretation variance.

#### Distinguish Input from Output Questions
Education has three levels: inputs (teaching), process (learning activities), outputs (what students learned):
- **Input questions**: "Were lectures well-organized?"
- **Process questions**: "Did you engage with course readings?"
- **Output questions**: "Can you now analyze a research paper critically?"

Most course evaluations over-weight inputs and ignore outputs. Assessment requires all three.

#### Ask About Specific Pedagogical Elements
Generic questions yield generic answers. Be specific:
- Instead of "Was grading fair?" ask:
  - "Were assignment expectations clear before you submitted work?"
  - "Did feedback on your work help you understand mistakes?"
  - "Were grading criteria consistent across assignments?"
- Each specific question yields actionable insight

#### Frame Questions as Improvement Opportunities
Students respond better when framed as helping, not judging:
- "What could the instructor do differently to help students learn better?" (collaborative)
- Better than: "What did the instructor do poorly?" (judgmental)

#### Include Self-Assessment Questions
Students' effort levels affect their experience:
- "On average, how many hours per week did you spend on this course outside of class?"
- "How often did you complete assigned readings before class?"
- These context questions help interpret satisfaction ratings

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Student Criticizes Teaching Method
Probe for specificity and alternatives:
- "You mentioned lectures weren't helpful. Can you describe a specific lecture where you felt lost?"
- "What would have helped you understand that concept better?"
- "Have you had courses where lectures worked well? What was different?"

Teaching criticism without specificity is venting. Specificity makes it actionable.

#### When Student Reports Learning Barrier
This is gold - prioritize understanding it:
- "You mentioned you struggled with the textbook. What specifically made it difficult?"
- "When you got stuck, what resources did you try?"
- "What would have helped you overcome that barrier?"

Learning barriers often affect multiple students but only one vocalizes it.

#### When Student Gives Perfect or Terrible Ratings Across All Items
Extreme uniformity suggests:
- Grade expectation contamination (check if this student is excelling or struggling)
- Lack of careful reflection
- Strong emotional reaction overriding nuance

In conversational surveys, the AI can probe: "I notice you feel very strongly across all areas. Was there any specific experience that shaped your overall view?"

#### When Student Conflates Instructor with Course Design
Students often don't distinguish instructor from curriculum:
- "You mentioned the assignments were busywork. Did the instructor design these assignments or are they required by the department?"
- "What specifically about the assignments felt unconnected to your learning?"

This distinction matters for actionable feedback.

#### When Student References Comparative Experiences
Comparisons reveal mental models:
- "You said this was the best course you've taken - what made it stand out?"
- "You mentioned X was better in your previous course. Can you describe what that instructor did differently?"

Comparative data reveals what students value implicitly.

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality Educational Survey Response Includes:
1. **Specific pedagogical moments**: "When we did the group debate on week 5, I finally understood the concept"
2. **Learning trajectory description**: "At first I was confused, but after the workshop it clicked"
3. **Outcome articulation**: "I can now do X that I couldn't do before"
4. **Constructive suggestions**: "If the instructor had done Y, it would have helped"
5. **Balanced perspective**: Acknowledges both strengths and areas for improvement

Example of LOW quality: "Instructor was great, I learned a lot"
Example of HIGH quality: "The instructor's use of real-world examples in week 3 helped me understand econometric models, which were abstract in the textbook. The problem sets reinforced this. I now feel confident analyzing economic data, which I couldn't do before."

#### Watch for Grade-Contaminated Responses
Responses that focus entirely on grading suggest grade expectations are driving evaluation:
- "The grading was too harsh"
- "Tests were harder than they should have been"
- "I deserved a better grade"

These may be legitimate, but probe for learning: "Despite the grading, did you feel you learned the material?"

#### Look for Growth Mindset vs. Fixed Mindset Language
Growth mindset responses are more reliable:
- Growth: "This course challenged me and I had to develop new study strategies"
- Fixed: "I've never been good at math so this course was impossible"

Fixed mindset responses may reflect student's self-concept more than teaching quality.

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Don't Ask About Instructor Personality Traits
Personality is not pedagogy:
- Bad: "Is the instructor enthusiastic?"
- Bad: "Is the instructor approachable?"
- Good: "Does the instructor respond helpfully to questions?"
- Good: "Does the instructor create opportunities for student participation?"

Focus on behaviors that affect learning, not personality characteristics.

#### Don't Use Course Evaluations Alone for Personnel Decisions
Student evaluations are one data source, not the only source:
- They correlate weakly with learning gains
- They are biased by factors unrelated to teaching quality
- They should be triangulated with: peer observation, teaching portfolios, learning outcome data, enrollment trends

Over-reliance on student evaluations creates perverse incentives (grade inflation, reducing rigor).

#### Don't Compare Scores Across Disciplines
A 4.2 in organic chemistry is not equivalent to a 4.2 in art history:
- Disciplines have different baseline ratings
- Students have different expectations
- Content difficulty varies

Always compare within-discipline or control for discipline effects.

#### Don't Ask Students to Evaluate Teaching Methods They Can't Observe
Students can't evaluate:
- Currency of syllabus content (they don't know the field)
- Alignment with department learning goals (they don't know the goals)
- Instructor's scholarly expertise (they're not experts)

Students CAN evaluate:
- Clarity of explanations
- Responsiveness to questions
- Organization of course materials
- Whether they learned what was promised

#### Don't Ignore Suggestions from Outlier Students
The student struggling the most often reveals barriers others don't mention:
- "I had no background in statistics and felt lost from day one"

This might reveal an unexamined prerequisite assumption.

Similarly, the top student's frustrations reveal ceiling effects:
- "I already knew most of this material; I wanted more advanced applications"

#### Don't Use Agree/Disagree Scales for Learning Outcomes
Learning outcomes should be self-assessment of competency:
- Bad: "I agree that I learned critical thinking skills" (conceptually weird)
- Good: "How confident are you in your ability to critically analyze research claims?"
  - Not at all confident / Slightly confident / Moderately confident / Very confident / Extremely confident

#### Don't Administer Evaluations Only at Course End
End-of-term evaluations can't improve the current students' experience:
- Add mid-term "check-in" surveys for formative feedback
- Use "minute papers" (1-minute reflection prompts) throughout the semester
- Create feedback loops that students can see affecting the course

## Domain 6: Civic Engagement & Public Opinion

### Core Principle
Public opinion polling is fundamentally about giving every individual in a population an equal voice on civic issues. Unlike market research (where sampling can be convenience-based) or customer feedback (where participants self-select), public opinion research carries a democratic obligation: the sample must accurately represent the population, and the questions must not bias responses. The methodology exists in service of representing collective sentiment, not individual perspectives.

The second core principle: stated opinions are influenced by question wording, order, and framing more than in any other survey domain. A single word change can swing results by 10-20 percentage points. This domain demands obsessive attention to neutrality and transparency.

### Creation Process for Public Opinion Surveys

#### 1. Define the Population and Sampling Frame
Before writing questions, crystallize exactly whose opinion you're measuring:
- **Geographic population**: Local (city), regional (state), national, or international?
- **Eligible population**: Registered voters, likely voters, all adults, all residents?
- **Sampling frame**: How will you reach them? (Phone lists, address lists, voter files, online panels)
- **Sample size calculation**: Based on desired confidence level (usually 95%) and margin of error (usually ±3-4%)
  - For population over 100,000: ~1,000 respondents gives ±3% margin of error
  - For tighter precision (±2%): ~2,400 respondents needed
  - For subgroup analysis: multiply needed sample by number of key subgroups

#### 2. Determine Representative Sampling Strategy
Public opinion surveys REQUIRE probability sampling (not convenience sampling):
- **Random sampling**: Every person has equal chance of selection (gold standard)
- **Stratified sampling**: Divide population into demographic strata (age, gender, region), then randomly sample within each
  - Ensures key groups aren't under-represented
  - Common strata: age, gender, race/ethnicity, education, geography
- **Weighting plan**: Even with good sampling, some groups respond more than others
  - Decide upfront: what demographics will you weight to match population?
  - Source population benchmarks: Census, voter files, previous elections
- **Multi-mode approach**: Combine phone (landline + cell), online, and mail to reduce coverage bias

#### 3. Establish Neutrality Guidelines
Every question must withstand scrutiny from opposing viewpoints:
- **Devil's advocate test**: Would both sides of an issue call this question fair?
- **Word choice audit**: Remove emotionally charged terms
  - Bad: "estate tax" vs "death tax" (same thing, different framing)
  - Good: "tax on inherited estates worth more than $X"
- **Balanced presentation**: If mentioning arguments for, also mention arguments against
- **Sponsor disclosure**: If poll sponsored by advocacy group, disclose upfront (affects responses)

#### 4. Pilot Test with Cognitive Interviews
Public opinion questions often mean different things to different people:
- Recruit 8-12 people demographically similar to target population
- Ask them to "think aloud" while answering questions
- Probe: "What does this question mean to you?" "How did you arrive at your answer?"
- Identify:
  - Words with multiple interpretations
  - Questions requiring information respondents don't have
  - Response options that don't fit their real views
- Iteratively revise until comprehension is >90%

#### 5. Design Question Flow for Context Management
Order effects are massive in public opinion research:
- **General before specific**: "Overall approval of president" before "approval of specific policy"
  - Specific questions prime attitudes that contaminate general questions
- **Unaided before aided**: "What issues are most important to you?" before showing list
  - Lists anchor and constrain responses
- **Demographic questions last**: Unless used for screening
  - Starting with race/gender/party primes group identity
- **Rotate response options**: Prevent primacy (first option) and recency (last option) bias
- **Randomize question blocks**: When testing multiple issues, randomize order across respondents

### Question Construction Principles

#### Use Neutral, Precise Language
Every word choice affects responses:
- **Avoid loaded terms**: "Government spending" vs "investing in services" vs "taxpayer money"
  - Test: Does this word have positive/negative connotation?
- **Be specific about tradeoffs**: "Would you support increasing education funding if it meant higher taxes?"
  - Not: "Would you support increasing education funding?" (who says no?)
- **Define ambiguous terms**: "Affordable housing" means different things to different people
  - Instead: "Housing affordable to families earning less than $X/year"

#### Provide Balanced Response Options
Don't make one side easier to choose:
- **Symmetric scales**: Equal positive and negative options
  - Good: Strongly oppose / Somewhat oppose / Somewhat support / Strongly support
  - Bad: Oppose / Somewhat support / Strongly support (asymmetric)
- **Include neutral option thoughtfully**:
  - "No opinion" / "Don't know" / "Neither support nor oppose"
  - Pro: Captures genuine ambivalence
  - Con: Provides easy out for avoiding thought
  - Best practice: Offer as explicit option, don't force a choice
- **Avoid agree/disagree for opinions**: These produce acquiescence bias (people tend to agree)
  - Bad: "Do you agree or disagree that [policy] should be implemented?"
  - Good: "Do you support or oppose [policy]?"

#### Handle "Don't Know" Strategically
Legitimate uncertainty vs. cognitive laziness:
- **For factual questions**: Always offer "Don't know"
  - "Do you know if your state requires voter ID?" → many genuinely don't know
- **For opinion questions**: Consider forced choice after soft probe
  - "Do you support or oppose X?"
  - If "Don't know": "If you had to choose, which way do you lean?"
  - Captures leaners vs. truly neutral
- **Report both**: "35% support, 28% oppose, 37% no opinion/don't know"

#### Use Validated Question Wording from Major Pollsters
Don't reinvent the wheel for standard questions:
- **Presidential approval**: "Do you approve or disapprove of the way [name] is handling his/her job as president?"
  - Standard wording allows comparison across time and polls
- **Issue importance**: "Which of these would you say is the most important issue facing the country today?"
  - Consistent wording enables trend tracking
- Sources for validated questions: Pew Research, Gallup, ANES (American National Election Studies)

#### Measure Intensity, Not Just Direction
Strength of opinion matters as much as position:
- **Intensity scales**: "Do you feel strongly about this, or not so strongly?"
- **Certainty questions**: "How certain are you of this opinion: very certain, somewhat certain, not too certain?"
- **Behavioral intent**: "Would you contact your representative about this issue?"
  - Behavioral likelihood indicates intensity better than self-reported strength

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Respondent Asks for Clarification
Public opinion surveys must be standardized—everyone gets the same stimulus:
- **Do not explain or elaborate**: "I need to read the question exactly as written. Let me read it again."
- **Do not provide examples**: This introduces new information inconsistently
- **Exception**: If terminology is genuinely technical, provide standard definition to ALL respondents
- Note: High clarification requests signal poor question design—flag for revision

#### When Respondent Says "I Don't Follow Politics/News"
This is legitimate and must be captured:
- **Do not push**: "That's perfectly fine. Would you say you have no opinion on this?"
- **Offer "don't know" explicitly**: Make it easy to opt out of topical questions
- **Screen with awareness questions**: "Have you heard about X?" before asking opinions on X
  - Only ask opinion questions to those with awareness
  - Report: "Among those aware of X, Y% support..."

#### When Respondent Wants to Explain Their Answer
Allow limited elaboration for data quality:
- Public opinion polls are usually quantitative, but qualitative flags are valuable
- "Thank you for that context" but don't engage in debate
- If reason is important, include structured follow-up: "What is the main reason you feel this way?"
  - Provide categorized response options, not pure open-end (for consistency)

#### When Respondent Expresses Strong Emotion
Acknowledge but remain neutral:
- "I understand this issue is important to you"
- Do not agree or disagree with their position
- Do not sympathize with their perspective (biases future responses)
- Maintain professional neutrality: you're a neutral recorder, not an ally

#### When Detect Satisficing (Speeding, Straightlining)
Some respondents give low-effort responses:
- **Straightlining**: Choosing same response for all questions
- **Speeding**: Answering too quickly to have read questions
- **AI should flag these patterns** for data quality review
- In conversational surveys, can insert attention check: "For this question, please select 'somewhat agree'"

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality Public Opinion Response Includes:
1. **Consistent positions across related questions**: Support for X aligns with related policy Y
2. **Appropriate certainty**: Strong opinions on high-salience issues, uncertainty on low-salience
3. **Reasonable response time**: Not too fast (didn't read) or too slow (confused)
4. **Coherent partisan patterns**: Liberal positions cluster, conservative positions cluster
5. **Thoughtful "don't know"**: Used selectively for genuinely unfamiliar topics, not all questions

#### Red Flags in Public Opinion Data:
- **Unanimous agreement across all questions**: Satisficing or not reading
- **Inconsistent partisan alignment**: Says they're liberal but holds all conservative positions (possible, but rare—flag for review)
- **No "don't know" responses**: Either extremely engaged citizens or not thoughtful
- **All "strongly" responses**: Not differentiating between issues

#### Demographic Representativeness Check:
- Compare sample demographics to population benchmarks
- **Over-represented groups**: High education, high income, older adults (they answer surveys more)
- **Under-represented groups**: Young adults, low education, minorities
- Apply weights to correct, or note limitations in reporting

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Don't Use Leading or Double-Barreled Questions
These are cardinal sins in public opinion research:
- **Leading**: "Do you agree that the disastrous policy should be reversed?"
  - Contains evaluative language ("disastrous")
- **Double-barreled**: "Do you support reducing crime and increasing police funding?"
  - Two questions: crime reduction + police funding
  - Someone might support one but not the other

#### Don't Ask Hypotheticals Without Context
People can't predict their opinions on unfamiliar scenarios:
- Bad: "If there were a proposal to do X, would you support it?"
- Good: "There is a proposal to do X. Supporters say [argument]. Opponents say [argument]. Do you support or oppose?"
- Provide enough info for informed opinion without biasing the response

#### Don't Forget to Rotate Response Options
Order effects are huge:
- **Primacy bias** (for self-administered): First option chosen more often
- **Recency bias** (for interviewer-read): Last option chosen more often
- **Solution**: Randomize order of candidate names, policy options, list items
- Exception: Ordinal scales (strongly disagree to strongly agree) have inherent order

#### Don't Survey Unrepresentative Samples and Call Them "Public Opinion"
Self-selected online polls are not public opinion:
- Reddit polls, Twitter polls, website polls = self-selected
- Visitors to conservative/liberal websites = ideologically skewed
- Must be transparent: "Non-probability sample of X" vs "Representative sample of Y"
- **Only probability samples can be generalized to populations**

#### Don't Split Sample Sizes Too Thinly
If you have 1,000 respondents and 10 subgroups, each subgroup has ~100:
- Margin of error for n=100 is ±10% (too wide for precise estimates)
- **Rule of thumb**: Need 300+ per subgroup for reliable subgroup analysis
- Either increase total sample or limit subgroup breakdowns

#### Don't Ignore Weighting
Even with perfect sampling, some groups respond more/less:
- Without weighting: Over-representing engaged, educated, older citizens
- **Must weight to match population demographics**
- Common weights: Age, gender, race, education, region
- Report: "Weighted to match 2020 Census demographics"

#### Don't Ask About Unfamiliar Topics Without Awareness Filter
Forcing opinions on topics people haven't heard of produces garbage data:
- Always include: "Before this survey, had you heard about X?"
- Only ask opinion questions to those aware
- **Response option**: "Haven't heard enough to say" as explicit choice

#### Don't Change Question Wording Mid-Tracking Study
If tracking trends over time, keep questions identical:
- Even small wording changes break comparability
- "Climate change" vs "global warming" = different responses
- "Obamacare" vs "Affordable Care Act" = different support levels
- If you must change, run both versions in one wave to calibrate

## Domain 7: Scientific & Academic Research

### Core Principle
Scientific research surveys operate under the highest standards of methodological rigor, ethical oversight, and epistemological scrutiny. Unlike applied research domains (CX, market research, organizational surveys) where pragmatic insights drive decisions, academic research surveys must contribute to generalizable knowledge, withstand peer review, and adhere to strict ethical protocols enforced by Institutional Review Boards (IRBs).

The core tension: research must balance internal validity (measuring what you claim to measure), external validity (generalizability), construct validity (theoretical soundness), and ecological validity (real-world applicability). Every design choice involves tradeoffs among these.

The second principle: participants are research subjects, not customers or stakeholders. Their rights, welfare, and dignity are paramount and protected by federal regulations (in the U.S., the Common Rule; internationally, Helsinki Declaration). This creates non-negotiable constraints that don't exist in other survey domains.

### Creation Process for Academic Research Surveys

#### 1. Ground in Theoretical Framework (Before Operationalization)
Academic surveys don't start with "what do we want to know?" but "what theory are we testing?"
- What is the theoretical construct you're measuring? (e.g., "self-efficacy," "social capital," "cognitive load")
- What is the operational definition? (How does the abstract construct manifest in observable indicators?)
- What is the rival hypothesis? (What alternative explanations must you rule out?)
- Are you conducting exploratory research (generating theory) or confirmatory research (testing theory)?

Example:
- Theory: Self-determination theory posits autonomy, competence, and relatedness drive motivation
- Constructs: Autonomy (sense of control), Competence (sense of effectiveness), Relatedness (sense of connection)
- Operational definition: Autonomy measured by frequency of choice, control over methods, input in decisions
- Rival hypothesis: Motivation driven by extrinsic rewards, not intrinsic psychological needs
- Design: Survey must measure both intrinsic and extrinsic factors to compare explanatory power

#### 2. Conduct Literature Review and Identify Validated Instruments
Academic research does not invent survey items casually:
- Search for existing validated scales measuring your construct
- Review psychometric properties: reliability (Cronbach's alpha), validity (convergent, discriminant, criterion)
- Prefer established instruments unless you're explicitly developing a new measure
- If modifying an existing scale, justify modifications and re-validate
- If creating new items, follow scale development protocols (e.g., DeVellis, Churchill paradigm)

Resources to check:
- Buros Mental Measurements Yearbook
- PsycTESTS database
- Published factor analyses in your field
- Handbook chapters on measurement

#### 3. Design for Construct Validity
Surveys must measure the theoretical construct, not confounds:
- **Convergent validity**: Does your measure correlate with other measures of the same construct?
- **Discriminant validity**: Does it NOT correlate with measures of different constructs?
- **Face validity**: Do items appear to measure what they claim? (Necessary but not sufficient)
- **Content validity**: Do items cover the full domain of the construct?
- **Criterion validity**: Does the measure predict real-world outcomes it should predict?

Design techniques:
- Include multiple items per construct (latent variable modeling)
- Include measures of theoretically related constructs (for convergent validity tests)
- Include measures of unrelated constructs (for discriminant validity tests)
- Include objective outcome measures when possible (not just self-report)

#### 4. Address IRB Requirements from the Start
IRB approval is not an afterthought; it shapes survey design fundamentally:
**Informed Consent Elements Required:**
- Purpose of research (in lay terms)
- Duration of participation
- Procedures involved
- Risks and benefits
- Voluntary nature of participation
- Right to withdraw at any time without penalty
- Confidentiality protections
- Contact information for questions and concerns
- IRB contact for rights violations

**Risk Assessment:**
- Minimal risk: Risks no greater than ordinary daily life
- More than minimal risk: Requires full board review, enhanced protections

**Vulnerable Populations** (require additional protections):
- Children (under 18)
- Prisoners
- Pregnant women
- People with cognitive impairments
- Economically disadvantaged
- Students (if researcher has power over them)

**Data Protection Requirements:**
- How will data be stored? (Encrypted? Password-protected?)
- Who will have access?
- How long will data be retained?
- Will data be de-identified? Anonymized?
- Will data be shared with other researchers? (Requires explicit consent)

#### 5. Power Analysis and Sample Size Determination
Academic research requires statistical justification for sample size:
- Conduct a priori power analysis (use G*Power or similar)
- Specify: effect size you want to detect, significance level (α, usually .05), desired power (usually .80)
- For surveys: factor analysis requires 5-10 participants per item (minimum 100-200)
- For structural equation modeling: minimum 200, prefer 300+
- For rare populations: justify maximum achievable N and acknowledge limitations

#### 6. Pilot Testing and Cognitive Interviewing
Before full deployment, always pilot test:
- **Cognitive interviews**: Ask 5-10 participants to "think aloud" while completing survey
  - "What does this question mean to you?"
  - "How did you arrive at your answer?"
  - "Was anything confusing?"
- **Pilot survey**: Deploy to 30-50 people similar to target population
  - Check completion time
  - Calculate preliminary reliability (Cronbach's alpha)
  - Identify problematic items (low variance, skewness, missing data)
  - Conduct exploratory factor analysis
- **Revise before full deployment**

### Question Construction Principles

#### Use Multi-Item Scales for Latent Constructs
Single-item measures are unreliable for complex constructs:
- Bad: "How satisfied are you with your job?" (1-7 scale)
- Good: Job Satisfaction Scale (multiple items):
  - "I find real enjoyment in my work"
  - "Most days I am enthusiastic about my work"
  - "I feel fairly well satisfied with my job"
  - "I find satisfaction in my work"

Multiple items allow:
- Calculation of internal consistency (reliability)
- Averaging to reduce measurement error
- Factor analysis to confirm unidimensionality

#### Balance Positively and Negatively Worded Items
Prevents acquiescence bias (tendency to agree):
- Positive: "I feel energized by my work"
- Negative (reverse-scored): "I feel drained by my work"

However: Negatively worded items can create artifacts (method effects). Use judiciously.

#### Use Validated Response Scales
Don't invent response options; use established scales:
- **Likert-type**: Strongly Disagree, Disagree, Neither, Agree, Strongly Agree
- **Frequency**: Never, Rarely, Sometimes, Often, Always
- **Agreement** (better labels): Completely Disagree, Mostly Disagree, Slightly Disagree, Slightly Agree, Mostly Agree, Completely Agree
- **Include neutral midpoint** for attitudes (but not for behaviors)
- **Number of options**: 5-7 optimal (more than 7 shows no reliability gain)

#### Write Declarative Statements, Not Questions (Usually)
For scaled responses, statements work better than questions:
- Less good: "How much do you agree with remote work policies?"
- Better: "I am satisfied with the remote work policies" (Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree)

Statements create consistent response format across items.

#### Avoid Double-Barreled Items (Especially in Academic Research)
One item, one idea (this is even more critical than in applied research):
- Bad: "I am satisfied with my salary and benefits"
- Good: Two items: "I am satisfied with my salary" + "I am satisfied with my benefits"

Double-barreled items reduce construct validity because you don't know which component drives the response.

#### Write Items at Appropriate Reading Level
Academic surveys often use jargon inappropriate for general populations:
- Target 8th-grade reading level for general populations
- Use Flesch-Kincaid readability tests
- Avoid: "I exhibit high self-efficacy regarding technological adoption"
- Prefer: "I feel confident using new technology"

#### Match Item Content to Theoretical Definition
Each item must be defensible as an indicator of the construct:
- If measuring "burnout" (defined as emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, reduced accomplishment):
  - Valid item: "I feel emotionally drained from my work" (emotional exhaustion)
  - Invalid item: "My job is stressful" (stress ≠ burnout)

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Respondent Gives Inconsistent Responses
In academic research, inconsistency is data to flag, not fix:
- Do NOT confront them ("You contradicted yourself")
- Do NOT try to resolve the inconsistency in real-time
- Do include attention checks in survey design to detect careless responding
- Do flag inconsistent cases for sensitivity analysis (analyze with and without them)

#### When Respondent Skips Many Items
Missing data is methodologically significant:
- Probe gently: "I notice you skipped several questions. Is there anything I can clarify?"
- Do NOT pressure them to answer (violates voluntariness)
- If many respondents skip the same item, it's a measurement problem (revise for future)
- Use appropriate missing data handling in analysis (FIML, multiple imputation, not listwise deletion)

#### When Respondent Requests Clarification on Item Meaning
This reveals ambiguity - a measurement problem:
- You CAN clarify in pilot testing (this informs revision)
- You CANNOT clarify in actual data collection (creates inconsistency across participants)
- In actual survey, you must say: "Please interpret the question however makes sense to you"
- Document all clarification requests to revise items for next version

#### When Respondent Questions Research Purpose
Transparency is required:
- Provide the information from your informed consent
- But you may withhold specific hypotheses to avoid demand characteristics
- Acceptable: "We're studying work motivation"
- Not required: "We're testing whether intrinsic motivation predicts retention better than salary"
- After data collection, many IRBs require debriefing with full purpose disclosure

#### When Respondent Reports Distress from Survey Content
This is an ethical issue requiring immediate response:
- Stop the survey
- Provide support resources (counseling, hotlines, appropriate referrals)
- Allow them to withdraw without penalty
- This should have been anticipated in IRB application and consent form
- Document the incident and report to IRB if serious

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality Academic Survey Response Includes:
1. **Consistent response patterns** (passes attention checks)
2. **Appropriate variance** (not all 1s or all 7s)
3. **Logical coherence** (related items correlate as expected)
4. **Completion** (minimal missing data)
5. **Sufficient time spent** (not speeding through)

#### Red Flags Requiring Data Screening:
- **Straight-lining**: Same response to all items
- **Response time too rapid**: Completed survey faster than possible to read items
- **Failed attention checks**: "Please select 'Strongly Agree' for this item"
- **Logical impossibilities**: Reports 30 hours/day spent on an activity
- **Patterned responding**: Alternating 1-2-1-2-1-2 across items

These cases are typically excluded from analysis with justification in methods section.

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Don't Use Unpublished, Unvalidated Scales Without Justification
Creating ad hoc items undermines scientific credibility:
- If you MUST create items, follow scale development procedures:
  - Generate large item pool from theory and qualitative research
  - Expert review for content validity
  - Cognitive interviews for comprehension
  - Pilot test with factor analysis
  - Publish validation study before using in main research
- If using unpublished scale, cite dissertation/working paper and report psychometrics

#### Don't Conflate Correlation with Causation in Survey Research
Surveys are typically correlational, not causal:
- Cross-sectional surveys cannot establish causality (temporal precedence unclear)
- You can say: "X was associated with Y"
- You cannot say: "X caused Y" (unless longitudinal design with proper controls)
- Explicitly acknowledge this limitation in discussion section

#### Don't Ignore Response Bias
Multiple forms of bias threaten validity:
- **Social desirability bias**: Respondents answer in socially approved ways
  - Mitigation: Use validated social desirability scales (Marlowe-Crowne), anonymous responding
- **Acquiescence bias**: Tendency to agree regardless of content
  - Mitigation: Reverse-scored items, balanced keying
- **Common method bias**: Same respondent, same time, same survey inflates correlations
  - Mitigation: Multi-method design, temporal separation, Harman's single-factor test

#### Don't Sample Convenience Populations for Generalizable Claims
If you sample undergraduates, you can only make claims about undergraduates:
- Convenience samples limit external validity
- Be explicit: "In a sample of university students..." not "People generally..."
- If you claim generalizability, you need probability sampling

#### Don't Modify Validated Scales Without Re-Validation
Changing even one word can

 alter psychometric properties:
- If you modify a validated scale, you must:
  - Justify the modification
  - Re-calculate reliability
  - Ideally, re-validate with factor analysis
  - Acknowledge this as a limitation

#### Don't Forget to Register Pre-Registered Hypotheses
Increasingly, academic journals expect pre-registration:
- Register hypotheses, sample size, analysis plan before data collection
- Platforms: OSF.io, AsPredicted.org, ClinicalTrials.gov
- Distinguishes confirmatory from exploratory analyses
- Prevents HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known)

#### Don't Use Leading or Loaded Questions in Academic Research
This violates objectivity:
- Bad: "To what extent do you agree that the innovative and effective policy should be adopted?"
- Good: "To what extent do you support the proposed policy?"

Every word must be neutral and defensible.

## Domain 9: Demographic & Social Characterization

### Core Principle
Demographic and social characterization surveys serve a dual purpose: they describe populations systematically (who people are) and provide context for understanding other data (why patterns exist). Unlike opinion polls (which measure what people think) or customer surveys (which measure satisfaction), demographic surveys are fundamentally descriptive and classificatory. They create the baseline understanding of a population's composition that enables all other research.

The critical challenge: demographic questions ask about identity, status, and personal circumstances—topics that can be sensitive, invasive, or politically charged. The methodology must balance the need for accurate classification with respect for privacy, dignity, and self-determination. Poor demographic survey design doesn't just produce bad data; it can alienate respondents and perpetuate harm.

### Creation Process for Demographic Surveys

#### 1. Define the Characterization Purpose (The Foundation)
Before asking any demographic questions, clarify exactly why you need this data:

**Population Description** (Census-style):
- Goal: Comprehensive snapshot of who lives in a geographic area
- Use case: Resource allocation, policy planning, representation
- Breadth: Wide range of characteristics (age, race, income, housing, employment, education, family structure)
- Depth: Moderate (enough to classify, not exhaustive)

**Segmentation for Analysis** (Research supplement):
- Goal: Understand how experiences/opinions vary by demographic group
- Use case: "Do men and women respond differently to this policy?"
- Breadth: Narrow (only demographics relevant to research question)
- Depth: Sufficient for meaningful subgroup analysis (need 100+ per segment)

**Equity and Inclusion Assessment**:
- Goal: Identify disparities and underrepresented groups
- Use case: "Are we reaching diverse populations?" "Where are gaps?"
- Breadth: Focus on protected classes and marginalized identities
- Depth: High (nuanced categories, intersectionality)

**Service Eligibility Screening**:
- Goal: Determine who qualifies for programs/services
- Use case: "Does this person meet income requirements for assistance?"
- Breadth: Narrow (only eligibility criteria)
- Depth: High precision (exact income, household composition)

#### 2. Determine Minimum Necessary Data
Demographic questions are intrusive—only ask what you'll actually use:
- **The "So What?" Test**: For each demographic question, answer: "How will this specific data point be analyzed or reported?"
- If you can't answer, don't ask it
- **Avoid "Nice to Know"**: Don't collect demographics "just in case" or "for completeness"
- **Minimize Identifiability**: More demographics = easier to re-identify individuals
  - Example: "Asian female PhD in rural Montana aged 65+" might be 1 person
  - Limit granularity or offer aggregated reporting only

#### 3. Choose Inclusive, Respectful Categories
Demographic categories are not neutral—they reflect power, history, and identity:

**Race and Ethnicity**:
- Use current Census categories as baseline (for comparability)
- Allow multiple selections ("Select all that apply")
- Include "Prefer to self-describe" with write-in option
- Separate race from ethnicity (Hispanic/Latino is ethnicity, not race)
- Never use outdated or offensive terms
- Consider: Are you measuring self-identity, observed identity, or ancestry? (These differ)

**Gender**:
- Minimum: Male / Female / Non-binary / Prefer to self-describe / Prefer not to say
- Better: Include "Transgender" as separate option or ask two questions:
  - "What is your gender?" (current identity)
  - "Do you identify as transgender?" (Yes/No/Prefer not to say)
- Never assume gender from name or voice
- Avoid "Other" (implies abnormality); use "Another gender identity" or "Prefer to self-describe"

**Age**:
- Ranges vs. Exact year: Ranges feel less invasive but lose precision
  - For general analysis: 18-24, 25-34, 35-44, 45-54, 55-64, 65+
  - For precise analysis: "What is your age?" or "What year were you born?"
- Consider: Does "age" mean current age or age at time of event?

**Income**:
- Highly sensitive—always include "Prefer not to say"
- Use ranges, not exact amounts (reduces discomfort)
- Specify: Individual or household income? Before or after taxes?
- Provide context: "This helps us understand if our services are reaching people across income levels"
- Consider: Income alone doesn't capture wealth (assets, debt, generational wealth)

**Education**:
- Standard categories: Less than high school / High school or equivalent / Some college / Associate degree / Bachelor's degree / Graduate degree
- Consider international respondents: Not everyone has US-style education system
- Avoid: "Highest level completed" can be stigmatizing; use "What is your education level?"

**Disability Status**:
- Use social model, not medical model: "Do you have a disability?" not "What's wrong with you?"
- Offer categories if needed: Mobility / Vision / Hearing / Cognitive / Mental health / Chronic illness / Prefer to self-describe
- Always include "Prefer not to say"
- Explain purpose: "We ask to ensure our services are accessible to everyone"

#### 4. Establish Ethical Data Handling Practices
Demographic data is sensitive—commit to protection:
- **Anonymity vs. Confidentiality**: Be explicit about which you're offering
- **Data minimization**: Collect only what's necessary, delete when no longer needed
- **Aggregation thresholds**: Never report demographic breakdowns for groups smaller than 10-15 people (re-identification risk)
- **Secure storage**: Encrypt, limit access, audit trails
- **Transparency**: Explain who sees the data and how it's used

#### 5. Pilot Test for Comprehension and Sensitivity
Demographic questions can confuse or offend if poorly worded:
- Test with diverse respondents (different ages, races, education levels)
- Ask: "How did this question make you feel?" "Was anything unclear or offensive?"
- Watch for:
  - Categories that don't fit respondents' identities
  - Terms that are outdated or stigmatizing
  - Questions that feel invasive without clear purpose
- Iterate until 95%+ comprehension and no reports of offense

### Question Construction Principles

#### Lead with Purpose and Privacy
Always explain why you're asking before demographic questions:
- "We ask the following questions to ensure we're hearing from a diverse range of people. Your responses are confidential and will only be reported in aggregate."
- This builds trust and increases response rates

#### Use "Select All That Apply" for Identity Questions
People have multiple, intersecting identities:
- Race/ethnicity: Many people are multiracial
- Disability: People can have multiple disabilities
- Language: Many people speak multiple languages
- Don't force people into single boxes that don't fit

#### Provide "Prefer Not to Say" for Everything
Demographic questions should always be optional:
- Some people find certain questions invasive
- Some fear discrimination if they answer honestly
- Forcing responses produces false data
- Exception: If demographic is required for eligibility, explain why

#### Use Neutral, Inclusive Language
Avoid assumptions and stereotypes:
- Bad: "Are you the head of household?" (assumes traditional family structure)
- Good: "What is your relationship to other household members?"
- Bad: "What is your marital status?" (assumes marriage is norm)
- Good: "What is your relationship status?" with inclusive options (single, partnered, married, divorced, widowed, separated)

#### Order Demographic Questions Strategically
Placement affects response:
- **End of survey**: Most common placement (less threatening after rapport built)
- **Beginning**: If demographics are screening criteria (e.g., "Are you 18+?")
- **Never in the middle**: Breaks flow and feels random
- **Sensitive questions last**: Age/income at very end

#### Provide Context for Sensitive Questions
Explain why you need potentially invasive information:
- Income: "This helps us understand if our program reaches people across economic backgrounds"
- Race: "We ask to ensure equitable representation and identify disparities"
- Disability: "This helps us improve accessibility of our services"

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Respondent Questions Why You're Asking
This is legitimate—demographic questions can feel invasive:
- Provide the prepared explanation: "We ask to ensure we're hearing from diverse voices"
- Emphasize: "This is optional—you can skip any question you're not comfortable answering"
- Reassure: "Your responses are confidential and reported only in aggregate"
- Do not pressure or guilt

#### When Respondent's Identity Doesn't Fit Categories
This happens frequently—categories are imperfect:
- "I understand our categories might not perfectly fit your identity. Would 'Prefer to self-describe' work, or would you prefer to skip this question?"
- If they want to self-describe, allow free text
- Note: High rates of "self-describe" signal your categories need revision

#### When Respondent Gives Contradictory Demographic Information
Sometimes people misunderstand questions or categories:
- Example: Selects "Hispanic" for race (it's ethnicity) and doesn't select a race
- Gently clarify: "I notice you selected Hispanic/Latino. For the race question, which category best describes you? You can select Hispanic/Latino for ethnicity and also select a race."
- Don't accuse of error—frame as clarification

#### When Respondent Expresses Discomfort with Question
Validate and offer opt-out:
- "I understand this question may feel personal. It's completely optional—would you prefer to skip it?"
- Do not minimize their discomfort ("Oh, it's not a big deal")
- Do not pressure ("But we really need this data")
- Their comfort matters more than your data completeness

#### When Respondent Asks How Data Will Be Used
Be transparent and specific:
- "We'll use this to create a demographic profile of respondents, like '45% were ages 25-34, 55% were ages 35+'"
- "We'll analyze whether experiences differ by demographic group, like comparing responses by gender"
- "We will not share individual responses—only aggregate summaries"
- If you can't be specific, you shouldn't be collecting the data

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality Demographic Data Includes:
1. **High completion rates**: >85% of respondents answer demographic questions (signals trust and clarity)
2. **Low "Prefer not to say" rates**: <15% opt out (signals questions feel appropriate)
3. **Rare use of "Other/Self-describe"**: <5% (signals categories fit most people)
4. **Demographic distribution matches known population**: If surveying US adults, age/race/gender should roughly match Census
5. **Consistent responses**: If asked related questions (e.g., age and birth year), they align

#### Red Flags in Demographic Data:
- **High skip rates on specific questions**: That question is too invasive or unclear
- **Many "Prefer not to say"**: Respondents don't trust you or don't understand why you're asking
- **Overuse of "Other"**: Your categories don't fit the population
- **Demographic skew**: If 80% are college-educated but population is 40%, you have sampling bias
- **Contradictory responses**: Age 25 but "retired" employment status (possible but rare—check for confusion)

#### Representativeness Assessment:
Compare your sample to population benchmarks:
- **Source benchmarks**: Census, voter files, previous representative surveys
- **Key demographics**: Age, gender, race/ethnicity, education, geography
- **Acceptable deviation**: ±5 percentage points for major groups
- **If skewed**: Apply weights or acknowledge limitations ("Our sample skews younger/more educated than general population")

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Don't Use Outdated or Offensive Terminology
Language evolves—stay current:
- Bad: "Oriental" (outdated, offensive)
- Good: "Asian" or specific ethnicity (Chinese, Korean, etc.)
- Bad: "Handicapped" (outdated)
- Good: "Person with a disability" or "Disabled person" (both acceptable, ask preference)
- Consult: Style guides from advocacy organizations (GLAAD for LGBTQ+, NCDJ for disability)

#### Don't Force Binary Choices for Non-Binary Identities
Gender, race, and other identities are not always binary:
- Bad: "Male or Female" with no other options
- Good: "Male / Female / Non-binary / Prefer to self-describe / Prefer not to say"
- Bad: "White or Non-white"
- Good: Full range of racial/ethnic categories with "Select all that apply"

#### Don't Ask Demographic Questions Without Explanation
Unexplained demographic questions feel invasive and random:
- Always include a preamble: "The following questions help us understand who we're hearing from"
- For sensitive questions, explain specific purpose
- If you can't explain why you need it, don't ask it

#### Don't Assume Demographics from Names, Voices, or Appearances
Never infer demographics—always ask:
- Don't assume gender from name or voice
- Don't assume race from appearance or surname
- Don't assume age from appearance
- Self-identification is the gold standard

#### Don't Create Overly Granular Categories That Enable Re-Identification
More detail = easier to identify individuals:
- Bad: "What is your exact age, ZIP code, occupation, and employer?"
  - This combination could identify a single person
- Good: Use ranges (age brackets, region instead of ZIP) or limit demographic questions
- **Rule**: If demographic combination could identify <10 people, it's too granular

#### Don't Use "Other" as a Catch-All
"Other" implies abnormality or unimportance:
- Bad: "Male / Female / Other"
- Good: "Male / Female / Non-binary / Another gender identity / Prefer not to say"
- "Other" is lazy design—think about who you're excluding and create appropriate categories

#### Don't Ask About Protected Characteristics Unless Legally Permitted and Necessary
Some demographic questions are illegal in certain contexts:
- **Employment**: In many jurisdictions, can't ask age, race, gender, disability, religion during hiring (discrimination risk)
- **Housing**: Can't ask about familial status, national origin in some contexts
- **Know the law**: Consult legal counsel for employment, housing, credit, insurance surveys

#### Don't Report Small Demographic Subgroups
Small groups can be re-identified:
- **Minimum reporting threshold**: 10-15 people per demographic cell
- If you have only 3 transgender respondents, don't report "Transgender: 67% agree"
  - Instead: Combine into larger category ("Gender minorities") or suppress ("Insufficient sample")
- Protects privacy and avoids misleading statistics (small samples have huge margins of error)

#### Don't Collect Demographics You Won't Analyze
Every demographic question should have a clear analytical purpose:
- If you're not going to break down results by income, don't ask about income
- If you're not comparing across education levels, don't ask about education
- Unused data is wasted respondent burden and privacy risk

#### Don't Use Demographic Data to Discriminate
This should be obvious but bears stating:
- Demographic data is for understanding and equity, not exclusion
- Never use demographic data to deny services, opportunities, or fair treatment
- If you're collecting demographics for compliance (e.g., EEO reporting), keep separate from decision-making data

## Domain 10: Infrastructure & Systems Performance

### Core Principle
Infrastructure and systems performance surveys measure the technical health, reliability, and efficiency of IT systems, networks, applications, and operational infrastructure. Unlike customer experience surveys (which measure user satisfaction) or market research (which explores preferences), infrastructure surveys are fundamentally diagnostic and operational. They answer: "Is the system working as designed?" "Where are the bottlenecks?" "What's at risk of failure?"

The critical distinction: Infrastructure surveys are conducted with technical stakeholders (IT staff, system administrators, DevOps engineers, service managers) who have operational expertise. Respondents understand technical terminology, have access to performance metrics, and can diagnose system issues. The methodology must balance technical precision with actionable insights for decision-makers.

### Creation Process for Infrastructure Surveys

#### 1. Define the Assessment Scope and Objectives
Before designing questions, clarify what systems and what aspects you're evaluating:

**System Health Assessment**:
- Goal: Comprehensive evaluation of infrastructure reliability and performance
- Scope: Servers, networks, databases, applications, storage, security systems
- Metrics: Uptime, latency, error rates, capacity utilization, incident frequency
- Stakeholders: IT operations, infrastructure teams, CIO/CTO

**Incident Post-Mortem**:
- Goal: Understand root causes and impacts of specific outages or failures
- Scope: Affected systems, timeline, contributing factors, response effectiveness
- Metrics: Downtime duration, MTTR (Mean Time To Repair), user impact, financial cost
- Stakeholders: Incident responders, affected teams, leadership

**Capacity Planning and Forecasting**:
- Goal: Identify resource constraints and predict future needs
- Scope: CPU, memory, storage, network bandwidth, database connections
- Metrics: Peak utilization, growth trends, headroom, bottlenecks
- Stakeholders: Infrastructure architects, capacity planners, finance (for budgeting)

**Service Level Agreement (SLA) Compliance**:
- Goal: Measure whether systems meet defined performance standards
- Scope: Response times, availability, throughput, error rates
- Metrics: SLA targets vs. actual performance, breach frequency, penalties
- Stakeholders: Service owners, account managers, customers

**IT Service Management (ITSM) Effectiveness**:
- Goal: Evaluate processes for managing IT services (ITIL framework)
- Scope: Incident management, change management, problem management, service desk
- Metrics: Ticket resolution time, first-time fix rate, change success rate, user satisfaction
- Stakeholders: Service desk, IT managers, end users

**Security and Compliance Monitoring**:
- Goal: Assess security posture and regulatory compliance
- Scope: Vulnerabilities, patch status, access controls, audit logs, compliance gaps
- Metrics: Vulnerability count, patch coverage, failed login attempts, compliance score
- Stakeholders: Security team, compliance officers, auditors

#### 2. Identify Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and Metrics
Infrastructure surveys should align with established monitoring frameworks:

**Availability Metrics**:
- Uptime percentage (e.g., 99.9% = "three nines")
- Downtime duration (planned vs. unplanned)
- MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures)
- MTTR (Mean Time To Repair/Recovery)

**Performance Metrics**:
- Response time / Latency (milliseconds)
- Throughput (transactions per second, requests per minute)
- Resource utilization (CPU %, memory %, disk I/O, network bandwidth)
- Queue depth / Backlog

**Reliability Metrics**:
- Error rate (% of failed requests/transactions)
- Incident frequency (number of incidents per month)
- Severity distribution (P1/P2/P3/P4 incidents)
- Repeat incident rate (same issue recurring)

**Capacity Metrics**:
- Current utilization vs. maximum capacity
- Headroom (available capacity before saturation)
- Growth rate (month-over-month, year-over-year)
- Projected time to capacity exhaustion

**User Experience Metrics** (from IT perspective):
- Ticket volume (help desk requests)
- User-reported issues (slow performance, errors, access problems)
- Application responsiveness (page load time, transaction completion time)

#### 3. Determine Respondent Expertise Level
Infrastructure surveys require technical knowledge—tailor questions accordingly:

**For System Administrators / DevOps Engineers**:
- Can answer: Specific error codes, log patterns, configuration details, performance metrics
- Use: Technical terminology, precise metrics, diagnostic questions
- Example: "What percentage of API requests returned 5xx errors in the past week?"

**For IT Managers / Service Owners**:
- Can answer: SLA compliance, incident trends, resource allocation, budget impacts
- Use: Business-oriented language, trend questions, prioritization questions
- Example: "Which systems pose the highest risk to business operations if they fail?"

**For End Users (Technical)**:
- Can answer: Application performance from user perspective, usability issues, workarounds
- Use: Experience-based questions, comparative questions, impact questions
- Example: "How often does the CRM system respond slowly enough to disrupt your work?"

**For Leadership (CIO/CTO)**:
- Can answer: Strategic priorities, budget constraints, risk tolerance, business alignment
- Use: High-level summaries, risk framing, ROI questions
- Example: "What infrastructure investments would have the highest business impact?"

#### 4. Establish Baseline and Benchmark Data
Infrastructure surveys are most valuable when compared to standards:
- **Internal baselines**: Historical performance of your own systems
  - "Is latency better or worse than last quarter?"
- **SLA targets**: Contractual or internal performance commitments
  - "Are we meeting our 99.9% uptime SLA?"
- **Industry benchmarks**: How do we compare to peers?
  - "Is our MTTR of 45 minutes fast or slow for our industry?"
- **Vendor specifications**: Are systems performing as designed?
  - "Is database throughput meeting vendor-promised specs?"

#### 5. Design for Actionability
Every infrastructure survey question should lead to a decision or action:
- **Diagnostic questions**: Identify what's broken or degraded
- **Prioritization questions**: Determine what to fix first
- **Root cause questions**: Understand why problems occur
- **Remediation questions**: Gather ideas for solutions
- If a question doesn't inform action, it's noise

### Question Construction Principles

#### Use Precise, Measurable Language
Avoid vague terms—use specific metrics:
- Bad: "Is the system fast?"
- Good: "What is the average API response time during peak hours?"
- Bad: "Does the database have problems?"
- Good: "How often does the database experience query latency exceeding 500ms?"

#### Ground Questions in Specific Time Windows
Infrastructure performance varies—specify when:
- "In the past 30 days, how many P1 incidents occurred?"
- "During peak business hours (9am-5pm), what is average CPU utilization?"
- "Since the last deployment (March 15), have error rates increased?"
- Avoid: "How is system performance?" (when? compared to what?)

#### Use Frequency Scales for Recurring Issues
For ongoing problems, frequency is more useful than yes/no:
- "How often does the application server require restart?"
  - Never / Rarely (monthly) / Sometimes (weekly) / Often (daily) / Constantly (multiple times per day)
- Better than: "Does the application server have stability issues?" (yes/no)

#### Separate Symptoms from Root Causes
Ask about observable problems first, then probe for causes:
- Symptom: "What performance issues have users reported?"
- Root cause: "What do you believe is causing these performance issues?"
- Don't conflate: "Is slow performance caused by insufficient memory?" (assumes cause)

#### Include Severity and Impact Assessment
Not all issues are equal—measure business impact:
- "When the payment gateway fails, what is the business impact?"
  - No impact / Minor inconvenience / Moderate disruption / Severe revenue loss / Critical business stoppage
- "How many users are affected when the VPN is down?"
  - <10 / 10-50 / 50-200 / 200-1000 / >1000

#### Use Conditional Logic for Efficiency
Don't ask about systems respondents don't manage:
- "Which systems do you directly manage?" [Checklist]
- Then: Only ask detailed questions about their selected systems
- Saves time and improves data quality (no guessing)

#### Ask About Monitoring and Visibility
Infrastructure problems often stem from lack of visibility:
- "Do you have real-time monitoring for this system?" (Yes/No)
- "How quickly are you alerted when this system fails?" (Immediate / Within 5 min / Within 30 min / Hours later / Not alerted)
- "What metrics do you lack visibility into?" (Open-ended)

### Interaction Patterns During Survey Execution

#### When Respondent Reports Critical Issue
Infrastructure surveys can uncover urgent problems:
- "You mentioned the database is at 95% capacity. Is this an immediate concern?"
- If yes: "Who should be notified about this?" (Escalate outside survey if needed)
- Document thoroughly: Exact metrics, timeline, affected systems
- Follow up: "What would you need to resolve this?"

#### When Respondent Gives Vague or Qualitative Answers to Quantitative Questions
Technical respondents should have access to metrics:
- Question: "What is average server CPU utilization?"
- Vague answer: "Pretty high"
- Probe: "Do you have access to monitoring dashboards that show exact percentages?"
- If no: This reveals a monitoring gap (valuable data)
- If yes: "Could you check and provide the specific number?"

#### When Respondent Blames Another Team or Vendor
Infrastructure issues often cross team boundaries:
- "You mentioned the network team's configuration caused the outage. Have you discussed this with them?"
- Probe for facts: "What specific configuration change led to the issue?"
- Avoid: Taking sides or validating blame
- Goal: Understand dependencies and communication gaps

#### When Respondent Requests Features or Resources
Infrastructure surveys often surface resource needs:
- "You mentioned needing more database capacity. What would be the impact of adding capacity?"
- "If you could upgrade one system, which would have the biggest operational impact?"
- Capture: Specific requests, business justification, estimated cost/effort
- These become inputs for budgeting and roadmap planning

#### When Respondent Reports Workarounds
Workarounds indicate systemic problems:
- "You mentioned restarting the server daily. How long has this been necessary?"
- "What would it take to fix the root cause instead of relying on this workaround?"
- Workarounds are technical debt—document them for prioritization

### Quality Indicators

#### High-Quality Infrastructure Survey Response Includes:
1. **Specific metrics**: "CPU averages 78% during peak hours" not "CPU is high"
2. **Time-bounded observations**: "Since the Q4 upgrade..." not "Sometimes..."
3. **Impact quantification**: "Affects 500 users, costs $10K/hour downtime"
4. **Root cause hypotheses**: "Likely due to memory leak in v2.3" not just "It's slow"
5. **Actionable recommendations**: "Increase database connections from 100 to 200"

#### Red Flags in Infrastructure Data:
- **All "Don't know" responses**: Respondent lacks access to systems or monitoring
- **Contradictory metrics**: Claims 99.9% uptime but reports daily outages
- **Blame without evidence**: "Vendor's fault" without specific technical details
- **Vague severity**: "It's bad" without quantifying impact
- **No monitoring data**: Can't answer basic performance questions (monitoring gap)

#### Validation Checks:
- **Cross-reference with monitoring tools**: Survey responses should align with automated metrics
- **Triangulate across respondents**: Multiple admins should report similar performance for shared systems
- **Compare to SLA baselines**: Reported performance should match SLA dashboards
- **Check for recency bias**: Recent incidents shouldn't dominate if overall performance is good

### Anti-Patterns and Mistakes to Avoid

#### Don't Ask Technical Questions of Non-Technical Respondents
Match question complexity to expertise:
- Bad: Asking end users "What is the database query execution plan?"
- Good: Asking DBAs about query plans, asking end users about application speed

#### Don't Conflate User Satisfaction with System Performance
These are different domains:
- Infrastructure survey: "What is average page load time?" (objective metric)
- Customer survey: "Are you satisfied with application speed?" (subjective experience)
- Both are valuable but measure different things

#### Don't Survey Without Access to Monitoring Data
If respondents can't access metrics, they'll guess:
- Before surveying, ensure respondents have access to:
  - Monitoring dashboards (Datadog, New Relic, Grafana, etc.)
  - Log aggregation tools (Splunk, ELK stack)
  - Incident tracking systems (PagerDuty, ServiceNow)
- If they lack access, fix that first

#### Don't Ask About Every System—Prioritize
Infrastructure is vast—focus on critical systems:
- Identify: Tier 1 (business-critical), Tier 2 (important), Tier 3 (nice-to-have)
- Survey deeply on Tier 1, lightly on Tier 2, skip Tier 3
- Alternatively: Let respondents select which systems they want to discuss

#### Don't Ignore the "People and Process" Dimension
Infrastructure problems are often organizational:
- Technical issue: "Database is slow"
- People issue: "DBA team is understaffed"
- Process issue: "No change management process, so deployments break things"
- Ask about: Staffing, training, documentation, communication, processes

#### Don't Survey Too Frequently
Infrastructure changes slowly—don't over-survey:
- **Quarterly**: Good cadence for ongoing health assessments
- **Post-incident**: Always survey after major outages
- **Post-change**: Survey after major upgrades or migrations
- **Annually**: Comprehensive infrastructure review
- Avoid: Monthly surveys (survey fatigue, no time for changes to manifest)

#### Don't Report Metrics Without Context
Raw numbers are meaningless without benchmarks:
- Bad: "Average latency is 250ms"
- Good: "Average latency is 250ms (target: <200ms, last quarter: 180ms)"
- Always include: Target/SLA, trend (better/worse), industry benchmark

#### Don't Ask "Why" Without First Establishing "What"
Diagnose symptoms before jumping to causes:
- First: "What performance issues are you experiencing?"
- Then: "What do you believe is causing these issues?"
- Not: "Why is the system slow?" (assumes it's slow, conflates symptom and cause)

#### Don't Forget to Close the Loop
Infrastructure surveys must lead to action:
- Share results with respondents: "Here's what we learned"
- Communicate action plan: "Based on your feedback, we're addressing X, Y, Z"
- Track progress: "Last quarter you reported latency issues—we've reduced latency by 40%"
- If you don't act on survey results, people stop responding

## Cross-Domain Synthesis

### What Makes These Domains Different from Each Other

1. **Stakes of Dishonesty**:
   - CX: Low (worst case: they don't buy again)
   - Market Research: Medium (you build the wrong product)
   - Workforce: High (careers, culture, retention at stake)
   - Education: Medium-High (faculty careers, student learning outcomes)
   - Academic Research: Highest (scientific validity, publishability, career advancement)

2. **Temporal Orientation**:
   - CX: Past (what just happened)
   - Market Research: Future (what might happen)
   - Workforce: Ongoing present (what's happening now)
   - Education: Recent past + cumulative learning (what happened this semester + what did I learn overall)
   - Academic Research: Timeless/generalizable (what is true across contexts)

3. **Expertise Asymmetry**:
   - CX: Respondent is expert on their experience
   - Market Research: Researcher knows product better; respondent knows their needs better
   - Workforce: Employees know daily reality; leaders know strategic context
   - Education: Students know their learning; educators know pedagogy
   - Academic Research: Researcher knows theory; respondent knows their own state

4. **Epistemological Standard**:
   - CX: Pragmatic (is this actionable?)
   - Market Research: Predictive (will this help us succeed in market?)
   - Workforce: Diagnostic (what needs fixing?)
   - Education: Developmental (how can teaching improve?)
   - Academic Research: Scientific (is this valid, reliable, generalizable knowledge?)

5. **Regulatory Oversight**:
   - CX: None (caveat emptor)
   - Market Research: Minimal (industry standards, GDPR for data)
   - Workforce: Medium (labor laws, DE&I regulations)
   - Education: Medium (FERPA, academic freedom, student rights)
   - Academic Research: Maximum (IRB, federal regulations, ethical review boards)

### Common Failure Modes Across Domains

Despite their differences, all 5 domains share failure patterns:
1. **Asking questions you can't act on** → survey fatigue
2. **Confusing satisfaction with quality** → measuring the wrong outcome
3. **Ignoring context** → data without meaning
4. **Over-reliance on quantitative scores** → missing the nuance
5. **Not closing the feedback loop** → breaking trust for future surveys
