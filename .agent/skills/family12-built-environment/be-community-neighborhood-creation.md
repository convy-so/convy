# Section 1 — Domain Identity

Community and Neighborhood Research is the systematic investigation of what it is actually like to live in a specific place — the accumulated daily experience of a residential area as felt by the people who inhabit it. It is distinct from urban planning assessments, property valuations, or infrastructure audits because it is concerned with the subjective, lived quality of place: how residents feel walking home at night, whether they know their neighbors, whether they feel pride or shame when they describe where they live to someone from outside.

The domain covers three interlocking dimensions that must be studied together. The physical realm — the streets, parks, buildings, and infrastructure that constitute the built environment. The social realm — the quality of relationships between residents, the sense of mutual recognition and community, and the degree to which different residents feel the neighborhood belongs to them equally. The service realm — the accessibility, quality, and reliability of the shops, transport links, and community facilities that make daily life possible or difficult. These three dimensions interact: a physically attractive neighborhood with poor social cohesion and failing services produces a fundamentally different lived experience from a physically unremarkable neighborhood with strong community bonds and accessible amenities.

This domain is not appropriate for studying the interior quality of residential dwellings — that belongs to Residential and Tenant Experience research. It is not appropriate for assessing city-wide policy performance — that belongs to Policy and Community Consultation research. It is not appropriate for evaluating workplace or office environments.

# Section 2 — Research Questions This Domain Can and Cannot Answer

## Questions this domain is equipped to answer

- What does it feel like to move through and inhabit this neighborhood in daily life across different times of day and different activities?
- Where in the physical environment do residents feel safe, welcomed, and at ease, and where do they feel exposed, unwelcome, or anxious?
- What is the quality of the social fabric — do residents feel they belong to a community or live in proximity to strangers?
- How accessible and adequate are the local services and amenities, and what is missing from daily life?
- What specific physical features, community nodes, or service touchpoints produce the strongest positive or negative sense of place?
- What is the resident's long-term commitment to the neighborhood, and what factors most drive them toward staying or leaving?
- How does the neighborhood's external reputation affect residents' sense of pride or embarrassment in living there?

## Questions this domain cannot answer

- Whether a specific infrastructure project represents good value for public money — redirect to Policy and Community Consultation research
- What non-resident visitors or commuters think of the neighborhood — this domain studies residents only
- Whether a specific property or dwelling is worth renting or buying — redirect to Residential and Tenant Experience research
- What city-wide policy decisions residents support or oppose beyond their immediate neighborhood context

# Section 3 — Brief Interrogation Guide

## 3.1 Neighborhood definition and scope

**Information required:** The specific geographic boundaries of the neighborhood being studied, how residents commonly understand those boundaries, and whether research covers the entire area or a specific sub-section.

**Complete answer:** "We are studying the Riverside District — bounded by the canal to the north, the high street to the east, the park to the south, and the railway to the west. Most residents know this area as Riverside. We are interested in the whole area but particularly the northern section near the new development."

**Incomplete answer:** "The local area." The agent must establish that the client and residents share a common understanding of what is being studied, because neighborhood identity is itself a research finding.

**Follow-up probe:** "When a resident says they live in this neighborhood, what geographic area do they typically mean? And is there any part that some residents consider a different neighborhood entirely?"

**Required or optional:** Required.

## 3.2 Commissioning entity and its relationship to residents

**Information required:** Who is commissioning the research, what organization they represent, what decision the findings will inform, and critically — whether they have any formal authority or financial relationship with the residents being studied.

**Complete answer:** "We are a housing association managing 400 units in the area. We want to understand whether residents' experience of the neighborhood supports or undermines satisfaction with our properties, ahead of a community programming investment decision. Residents know us as their landlord."

**Incomplete answer:** "We want to understand the neighborhood." The commissioning entity and its relationship to residents must be identified because it directly affects the social dynamics of the research and the Analytics agent's interpretation of any authority deference patterns in the data.

**Follow-up probe:** "Do residents have a direct relationship with your organization — as their landlord, their planning authority, or in another capacity? And will they know who commissioned this research?"

**Required or optional:** Required — blocking. See Constitutional Constraints.

## 3.3 Resident population profile

**Information required:** Tenure type distribution (owner-occupier, private rental, social housing), approximate demographics, typical length of residence, and whether meaningful population diversity warrants segmented analysis.

**Complete answer:** "Roughly 40% owner-occupiers, mostly long-term residents of ten-plus years; 30% private renters, younger with higher turnover; 30% social housing tenants, long-term with strong community ties. There is a significant South Asian community concentrated in the northern section."

**Incomplete answer:** "It's a mixed area." The tenure split is critical — owners, private renters, and social tenants experience the same neighborhood very differently and have different stakes in its future.

**Follow-up probe:** "Are there groups of residents whose experience of this neighborhood is likely to be significantly different from others — by how long they've been here, their housing situation, their background, or any other dimension?"

**Required or optional:** Required.

## 3.4 Known issues and trigger events

**Information required:** What the client already knows or suspects about neighborhood strengths and problems, and what has changed recently to prompt this research now.

**Complete answer:** "We've had a 30% increase in antisocial behavior complaints in eighteen months. We believe this is connected to the community center closure two years ago but we're not certain. Completion rates for our resident satisfaction surveys have also dropped."

**Follow-up probe:** "What are residents telling you informally — through complaints, community meetings, or social media — that you want this research to confirm or challenge? And is there any finding that would genuinely surprise you?"

**Required or optional:** Required. Known issues determine node weighting and let the Conducting agent anticipate sensitive terrain.

## 3.5 Physical context and recent changes

**Information required:** The physical character of the neighborhood and any significant recent or planned changes that residents are likely to have strong feelings about.

**Complete answer:** "Dense Victorian street grid, a public park at the center, a declining high street. A large mixed-use development is under construction on the former gasworks site on the northern edge. The community center closed two years ago. A new coffee shop and coworking space opened last year and has been controversial."

**Follow-up probe:** "In the past two or three years, what has changed physically in this neighborhood — what has opened, closed, been built, or been demolished — that residents are likely to reference in a conversation about their experience of the area?"

**Required or optional:** Advisory. Physical context enriches node weighting but is not blocking.

# Section 4 — Decision Map Interrogation

Neighborhood research frequently reveals problems the commissioning client cannot directly fix — infrastructure issues, social fragmentation, inadequate services — because responsibility lies with other actors. The agent must explicitly surface what is within the client's power before building the decision map.

## Interrogation framework

**For positive findings:**
"If the research shows strong community bonds and a genuine sense of place — that this neighborhood is functioning well as a community — what specifically changes for you? Is there a business case you're making, a program you'd continue, or a planning decision this would support?"

**For negative findings:**
"If the research identifies serious safety concerns, social fragmentation, or inadequate services — which of those could you directly address with your own resources? And which would require external actors whose cooperation you don't control?"

**For mixed findings:**
"Most neighborhoods have areas that work and areas that don't — by geography, by time of day, or by population group. If that's what we find, how do you prioritize? Is there a specific community, a specific location, or a specific problem type that matters most for your decision?"

## What a complete decision map looks like for this domain

Positive: "If social cohesion is strong and barriers are primarily physical rather than relational, we proceed with the community hub investment and use the findings to justify the business case to our board."

Negative: "If safety perception after dark is significantly low — particularly for women and young people — we commission a lighting audit of the identified streets and engage the local authority on a joint action plan within three months."

Mixed: "If the experience differs significantly between long-term residents and recent arrivals, we design a specific integration program for newer residents and use the segmented findings to argue for a community development worker post in next year's budget."

# Section 6 — Audience Model Interrogation Guide

## Psychographic dimensions to capture

**Tenure type and residential stake:** Owner-occupiers have financial and emotional investment in neighborhood quality and reputation. Private renters have immediate practical concerns and lower long-term commitment. Social housing tenants often have the deepest community ties but the least power to change their situation. These groups experience the same neighborhood very differently.

**Length of residence:** Long-term residents (ten or more years) carry a before-and-after comparison frame — they evaluate the neighborhood against what it was. Recent arrivals (under two years) compare against their expectations and their previous location.

**Identity-based experience position:** Residents from minority ethnic communities, residents with disabilities, older residents, and residents with young children often experience the same physical space very differently from the default population. These differences are frequently the most important findings in neighborhood research and the most sensitive to surface.

## Questions to surface these dimensions

"Do you own your home, rent privately, or are you in social or council housing?"

"How long have you lived in this area?"

"Has your sense of this neighborhood changed since you arrived — is it different now from when you first moved in?"

## Known sensitivity flags for this population

Residents who have experienced crime, harassment, or antisocial behavior may be reluctant to describe specific incidents or locations if they fear retaliation or do not trust the research will lead to action. Significant trust-building is required before safety node depth is accessible.

Residents from minority communities may have had experiences of discrimination, exclusion from community spaces, or differential treatment that shaped their neighborhood experience fundamentally. These require extended rapport before they emerge.

Residents in social housing may be cautious about criticizing the neighborhood if they believe it reflects on their tenure or housing options.

# Section 7 — Constitutional Constraints

## Blocking constraints

**Constraint B-1: The commissioning entity's relationship to residents must be identified and assessed.** If the commissioning entity is the residents' landlord, planning authority, or any organization with power over residents' circumstances, the trust dynamics of the research change fundamentally. The brief cannot proceed without this assessment recorded.

**Constraint B-2: The decision map must explicitly distinguish between what the client can directly change and what requires external actors.** A neighborhood research brief whose entire decision map consists of things the client cannot control is not a valid brief. The agent must probe until at least one directly actionable scenario is articulated.

## Advisory constraints

**Advisory A-1: If a significant physical change is planned or underway, record in expertState.** Planned development or service changes create specific audience psychology — residents may express opinions shaped by hope or anxiety about what is coming rather than their current experience only.

**Advisory A-2: If the population is significantly diverse, recommend segmented sessions.** A single session with one resident type is insufficient for a neighborhood with meaningfully different population groups. Record which segments are not covered by the planned research.

# Section 8 — Duration Calibration Guide

| Research focus | Minimum duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Single dimension only | 15 minutes | Narrow brief; maximum four active nodes |
| Full neighborhood experience | 22–28 minutes | Standard; all critical nodes active |
| Longitudinal comparison (before/after change) | 25–30 minutes | Additional change perception nodes |

NODE-CN-04b (evening safety) requires the most careful pacing in this domain. Residents who have experienced safety incidents need time to decide whether to disclose. Do not approach this node until at least ten minutes of rapport have been established, and never as an opening topic.
