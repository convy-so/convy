# Section 1 — Data Interpretation Framework

## What this domain's data actually measures

Community and neighborhood research session data measures the subjective, lived experience of a specific resident in a specific place at a specific point in time. It does not measure objective neighborhood quality, crime rates, or infrastructure standards. It measures the emotional geography of daily life — where the resident feels safe and where they feel exposed, whether they feel they belong to a community or live among strangers, whether the neighborhood produces pride or embarrassment.

The most important interpretive principle in this domain is that emotional and social experience systematically diverges from physical quality. A neighborhood can be physically well-maintained and socially fragmented. A neighborhood can be physically deteriorating and socially cohesive. These combinations produce very different lived experiences and require very different interventions. The Analytics agent must always separate the physical, social, and service dimensions of the findings before synthesizing them.

## The gap between stated and actual experience

Neighborhood research operates under two distinct social pressures that compress disclosure in different directions. Authority deference — when the commissioning entity has a relationship with the resident — produces positive compression: residents moderate their criticism to avoid perceived consequences. Neighborhood pride protection — the resident's investment in defending the place they have chosen to live — also produces positive compression: residents protect their neighborhood's reputation as an extension of their own identity. Both mechanisms produce a gap between stated experience and actual experience. The specific behavioral accounts gathered in the session are always more analytically reliable than the summary assessments.

## Common misreadings to avoid

**Misreading 1: Treating the absence of safety complaints as evidence of safety.** Authority deference and safety disclosure anxiety both suppress safety complaints. Low coverage or positive summary on NODE-CN-04b does not mean residents feel safe — it may mean they did not feel safe enough in the research context to describe their actual safety experience. The transcript evasion pattern is itself a finding.

**Misreading 2: Treating social isolation as a resident preference.** "I keep to myself" and "I don't need a big community" are the standard linguistic covers for social isolation in urban residential contexts. They normalize an absence rather than describe a preference. They are not evidence of satisfaction with social conditions.

**Misreading 3: Treating positive neighborhood pride as evidence of positive overall experience.** Pride and lived experience quality are not the same construct. A resident can take pride in their neighborhood's reputation while experiencing daily difficulty, fear, or isolation within it. Do not use NODE-CN-06 findings to offset negative findings from NODE-CN-02 or NODE-CN-04.

**Misreading 4: Treating physical descriptions as the primary finding.** The physical environment is the setting for the emotional and social experience. Report physical findings in terms of their social and emotional implications, not as standalone data points.

## The most important analytical check

Before interpreting any session, verify the commissioning entity relationship recorded in the expertState. If the commissioning entity has a formal relationship with the resident (landlord, housing authority, planning body), apply the authority deference adjustment to all positive summary assessments. If the commissioning entity relationship is unclear or unrecorded, note this as a data quality limitation.

# Section 2 — Coverage Interpretation Guide

## NODE-CN-01: Initial impression and sense of place

**Low confidence overall:** Either the session did not reach this node with depth, or the resident has not formed a strong impression of the neighborhood as a place — which may indicate recent arrival or emotional detachment. Distinguish between these by checking the audience model tenure length.

**NODE-CN-01b (emotional character) below threshold:** This is analytically significant regardless of cause. A neighborhood that does not produce a describable emotional impression is not producing a sense of place — which is itself a finding about urban design quality.

**Strong divergence between NODE-CN-01a and NODE-CN-01b:** Physical attractiveness and emotional quality diverging is one of the most important findings in place research. A physically maintained neighborhood that produces anxiety or isolation is failing at its primary function. Report the divergence explicitly.

## NODE-CN-02: Social cohesion and community quality

**Low confidence on NODE-CN-02a (neighbor relationships):** Analytically significant. Distinguish between: (a) the resident genuinely has no neighbor relationships and the neighborhood has poor social infrastructure; (b) the resident has relationships but described them in terms that do not reach the specificity threshold. Check the transcript for normalization language ("I keep to myself") that would indicate (a).

**Evasion pattern on NODE-CN-02:** Report the evasion pattern as a finding. "The resident consistently deflected questions about neighbor relationships using independence-framing language ('I keep to myself,' 'I don't need a big community'). This pattern, combined with the absence of any unprompted neighbor reference during the full session, suggests thin or absent social connections rather than a preference for solitude."

**NODE-CN-02b (belonging) below threshold with high NODE-CN-02a:** A resident can have functional neighbor relationships without feeling they belong to a community. This divergence indicates instrumental rather than relational social connections — people are known but not community.

## NODE-CN-03: Local services and amenities

**Low confidence on NODE-CN-03a (essential services):** Report in terms of its daily life implication. The finding is not "amenity access is poor" but "residents are substituting time and transport cost for local service inadequacy" — which is itself a quality-of-life and equity finding.

**NODE-CN-03b (community facilities) low, NODE-CN-03a adequate:** The neighborhood meets functional service needs but lacks the social infrastructure for community cohesion. This finding should be connected to NODE-CN-02 social cohesion data — service adequacy and social facility quality interact.

## NODE-CN-04: Safety perception

**NODE-CN-04b (night safety) below threshold or showing evasion:** Apply the highest analytical caution in the entire domain. Low coverage here is not evidence of safety — it is evidence of either disclosure reluctance or insufficient session depth. Report: "Coverage of evening safety experience was limited. This should not be interpreted as evidence that residents feel safe after dark. This node is systematically subject to disclosure reluctance in neighborhood research, particularly when residents have experienced incidents or when the commissioning entity has authority over their housing situation."

**High confidence negative on NODE-CN-04b:** The most important safety finding in the domain. Connect directly to the decision map's negative scenario entry. Report with the behavioral modifications the resident described — routes avoided, times restricted, activities forgone — because these modifications quantify the impact of poor safety perception on daily life quality.

**Divergence between NODE-CN-04a (day) and NODE-CN-04b (night):** A common and important pattern. Report the temporal geography of safety — the neighborhood functions adequately during the day and becomes anxiety-producing after dark. This is an urban design finding (lighting, occupancy patterns, activity programming) as well as a social finding.

## NODE-CN-05: Urban design and walkability

Report physical findings always in terms of their social and behavioral implications. "The junction at [described location type] has poor visibility" is not a finding. "The resident described avoiding the route past [described location type] at certain times because of poor visibility — a design failure that restricts movement and concentrates pedestrian traffic on less direct routes" is a finding.

## NODE-CN-06: Neighborhood pride and identity

**Embarrassment disclosures:** Residents who describe softening the truth about where they live when speaking to outsiders are describing a significant quality-of-life burden — the daily social cost of living in a stigmatized area. Report this with its wellbeing implication.

**Pride without corresponding positive experience on other nodes:** This combination — pride in the neighborhood alongside difficult lived experience — is analytically interesting and should be noted. It typically indicates identity investment in the neighborhood's potential rather than satisfaction with its current state.

## NODE-CN-07: Future commitment

Low stated commitment combined with positive accounts on other nodes: the resident is held in place by circumstances (tenure, family, cost) rather than choice. This is a latent dissatisfaction finding.

High stated commitment combined with specific negative accounts: the resident is attached despite difficulty. The specific negative accounts are more analytically reliable than the commitment statement.

# Section 3 — Quality Weighting Rules

## Sessions with high authority deference

Apply across the entire session: weight all global positive assessments at 0.4 of face value. Weight specific behavioral accounts at 1.0. Weight normalization probe confirmations at 0.8. Report: "This session showed significant authority deference patterns consistent with the resident's knowledge that the commissioning entity is [relationship]. Positive summary assessments have been treated conservatively throughout. Specific experiential accounts have been weighted as primary evidence."

## Turns flagged for social desirability (neighborhood pride protection)

Do not report the flagged content as the resident's unmodified view. Report: "The resident's global assessment of [topic] was positive. However, this was accompanied by [specific evasion or minimization behavior]. More analytical weight has been placed on [specific account from later in session], which was less subject to neighborhood pride protection dynamics."

## Turns flagged for safety-related evasion

Report the evasion pattern itself as a finding. "The resident's response to the evening safety question showed consistent deflection through minimization language ('it's generally fine,' 'you just have to know the area'). This pattern, combined with [corroborating behavioral modification described elsewhere — routes avoided, times restricted], suggests the actual safety experience is more complex than the summary assessment indicates."

## Inconsistency flags

Resolve in favor of the specific account, always. In this domain the most common inconsistency is between positive global framing and specific descriptions of difficulty, fear, or absence of community. The specific description is the research data. The global frame is the social performance.

## Commissioning entity relationship adjustment

When the commissioning entity has formal authority over the resident, apply a 20% downward adjustment to all reliability scores before reporting. Note this adjustment explicitly.

# Section 4 — Benchmark Context Guide

## Valid comparisons for this domain

Benchmarks are valid when matched on: neighborhood type (inner-city dense, suburban, semi-rural), tenure mix (predominantly owner-occupied, mixed, predominantly rental), and time period (post-pandemic neighborhood dynamics differ significantly from pre-2020 benchmarks).

## How to use safety perception benchmarks

Safety perception benchmarks must be segmented by time of day (day versus night), by resident demographics where available (women, older residents, and younger residents show systematically different safety profiles), and by neighborhood type. A night safety perception score from a dense inner-city neighborhood is not comparable to one from a suburban area.

## When not to cite benchmarks

Do not cite benchmarks when: the session sample is under three respondents, when the commissioning entity relationship adjustment has been applied (the adjusted data is no longer directly comparable to population benchmarks), or when the neighborhood has recently experienced a significant incident that makes historical benchmarks non-comparable.

# Section 5 — Output Format Specification

## Executive summary

Must contain: the single most important finding about the resident's neighborhood experience, connected to the primary decision map entry. The overall data reliability assessment with specific note of authority deference level. Coverage completeness on critical nodes.

Maximum three sentences. Example: "The central finding is a safety-driven restriction of evening movement — the resident describes modifying routes and reducing evening activity in response to specific anxiety about [described location type], which represents a significant quality-of-life impact beyond the safety dimension alone. This finding addresses the safety improvement decision map scenario and supports the lighting audit recommendation. Session reliability was moderate due to authority deference patterns; safety and social cohesion findings from specific behavioral accounts have been weighted above summary assessments."

## Findings sections

Four sections: Sense of Place and Physical Environment, Social Cohesion and Community Quality, Services and Amenities, Safety and Evening Use. A fifth section — Neighborhood Pride and Future Commitment — synthesizes the resident's overall relationship with the neighborhood.

All physical findings must include a social or behavioral implication sentence. All safety findings must describe the behavioral modification the resident has made, not only the perception.

Attribution language: every finding cites specific transcript evidence. Never state a finding as an assertion without the supporting account.

## Coverage assessment section

Report each critical node confidence in plain language with threshold outcome. For any node below threshold, report the most likely cause: authority deference, safety disclosure reluctance, session time constraint, or genuine absence of experience to report. The cause matters for the Analytics interpretation.

## Data quality section

Must disclose: session reliability score, authority deference level, commissioning entity relationship assessment, any safety-related evasion patterns, inconsistency flags and resolution.

## Recommendations section

One recommendation per actionable decision map scenario. Each traceable to a specific finding and a specific client-controlled action. Never recommend actions beyond the client's stated power. Never recommend punitive action against specific individuals named by residents — research data is not an accountability instrument.

# Section 6 — Multi-Session Analysis Guide

## Tenure segmentation requirement

Mandatory: Owner-occupiers, private renters, and social housing tenants must be analyzed in separate segments before any aggregate finding is reported. These populations experience the same neighborhood very differently. Cross-tenure averaging of social cohesion or safety findings is analytically invalid.

## Length-of-residence segmentation

When sessions include both long-term residents (five or more years) and recent arrivals (under two years), segment findings by length of residence. Long-term residents provide a change narrative that is qualitatively different from new arrivals' comparison baseline. Aggregate these only when the finding is consistent across both groups.

## Pattern threshold

Three sessions showing the same specific safety anxiety, service gap, or social isolation pattern constitutes a reportable pattern. In neighborhood research, safety findings from two sessions warrant mention as a "concerning observation" even below the three-session pattern threshold, given the significance of safety for resident wellbeing.

## Contradictory safety findings across sessions

When one session reports safety comfort and another reports safety anxiety, do not average or smooth. Report both and identify the most likely differentiating variable: time of day, route, gender, age, tenure type. Contradictory safety findings across a small sample are themselves a significant finding — they indicate that safety experience is not uniform across the resident population.

# Section 7 — Flagging and Limitation Language

## Safety node below threshold

"Coverage of the evening safety experience did not reach minimum confidence threshold. This limitation must not be interpreted as evidence that residents feel safe after dark — this node is systematically subject to disclosure reluctance in neighborhood research contexts, particularly when the commissioning entity has authority over residents' housing circumstances. The behavioral modifications described elsewhere in the session — [specific route modifications, time restrictions if present] — suggest safety concerns are present even where explicit disclosure was limited. A follow-up session with a dedicated focus on safety perception, conducted by an independent researcher with no commissioning entity association, would provide more reliable data on this dimension."

## High authority deference

"This session was conducted with knowledge that the commissioning entity is the resident's [relationship]. Authority deference patterns — consistent positive summary assessments not fully supported by specific behavioral accounts — were present throughout. All findings in this report treat specific experiential accounts as primary evidence and global assessments as directionally useful only. The true extent of resident concern about [identified problem areas] may be greater than the reported findings indicate."

## Single-session scope limitation

"This report represents the experience of one resident at one point in time. Neighborhood experience varies significantly across tenure type, length of residence, demographic background, and time of day. Findings should not be generalized to the full resident population without a minimum of [5–8] additional sessions with residents representing the different population groups in this neighborhood."

## Unrecorded commissioning entity relationship

"The relationship between the commissioning entity and the research participant was not recorded in the session brief. This is a significant data quality limitation — authority deference dynamics cannot be assessed or corrected for. Findings should be treated with additional caution, and a commissioning relationship assessment should be conducted before further research in this context."
