# Section 1 — Data Interpretation Framework

## What this domain's data measures

Workplace and office environment session data measures the behavioral and experiential relationship between a specific employee and a specific physical workspace — what they do there, what they avoid doing there, what drives them to come in or stay home, and what the gap is between the workspace's designed purpose and its actual function in the employee's working life. It does not measure attendance rates, occupancy utilization metrics, or organizational productivity. It measures the experiential determinants of those metrics.

The most important interpretive principle is the primacy of behavioral evidence over evaluative statements. An employee who says "the office works well for me" while describing a pattern of booking meeting rooms for solo focus work, using phone booths as a substitute for quiet zones, and reserving office days exclusively for meeting-heavy schedules is not describing a workspace that works well for them — they are describing a workspace they have learned to navigate through workarounds. The workaround behaviors are the research data. The evaluative summary is the social performance.

## The workaround as the primary finding

Spatial workarounds — employees consistently using spaces in ways other than their intended purpose — are the most analytically valuable observations in workplace research. They reveal the precise gap between designed intent and actual need more clearly than any evaluation question. When workarounds are present in the session data, they must be reported as primary findings with their design implication stated explicitly.

## Common misreadings

**Misreading 1: Treating the absence of explicit complaints as evidence of design success.** Return-to-office tension and office design loyalty both suppress explicit criticism. The workaround behaviors described in the session are more reliable evidence of design gaps than the absence of stated complaints.

**Misreading 2: Treating focus work frustration as a personal preference rather than a design finding.** When employees consistently describe difficulty achieving concentrated work in the open plan, this is a design finding — the ratio of collaborative to focus-work spaces does not match the actual work mode distribution. It must not be reported as individual preference variation.

**Misreading 3: Treating technology failure as an IT issue rather than a hybrid design finding.** AV failures and connectivity problems in meeting rooms are not isolated technical incidents — they are hybrid workplace design failures that create two-tier meeting experiences and deter office attendance. Report them as design findings with attendance implications.

**Misreading 4: Treating low attendance as an attitude problem rather than a product-market fit problem.** When employees describe the home environment as better than the office for their primary work mode, the office is failing its product-market fit — it is not offering something sufficiently valuable to justify the commute cost. This is a design problem, not a motivation problem.

# Section 2 — Coverage Interpretation Guide

## NODE-WO-01: Journey of use and spatial decision-making

**Rich journey data:** The specific-day journey narrative produces data across multiple nodes simultaneously. Analyze it first for the spatial map it reveals — which spaces were used, which were avoided, and what the logic was for each choice. The map reveals utilization patterns, spatial adequacy, and the employee's implicit hierarchy of space needs.

**Workaround identification in journey data:** Any moment in the journey where the employee used a space for a purpose other than its intended design function is a workaround. Catalog all workarounds in the journey narrative before interpreting any other node.

## NODE-WO-02: Focus work support

**This is the most commonly deficient node in contemporary office design.** Organizations that converted to collaboration-primary open-plan offices in the 2015–2020 period systematically under-provisioned for focused individual work, and the return-to-office push post-pandemic has collided with this legacy design gap. When NODE-WO-02 shows deficiency, connect directly to NODE-WO-07 (hybrid decision) — focus work inadequacy is the single most commonly cited reason for preferring home over office.

**Focus work workarounds:** Phone booth use for solo work, meeting room booking for individual focus sessions, early arrival or late-evening office use to access quiet time — these are all focus work workarounds. Each one is a design recommendation: the employee is showing you what space they need by what they substitute for it.

**Attendance impact test:** The critical analytical question for NODE-WO-02 is whether focus work inadequacy directly affects attendance decisions. If the respondent described choosing to work from home on days with heavy individual work requirements, the focus work gap is an attendance driver — a design finding with commercial implications.

## NODE-WO-03: Collaboration space effectiveness

**Quality versus availability:** Distinguish between respondents who describe collaboration spaces as inadequate in quantity (can't get a room when needed) versus inadequate in quality (get the room but the experience inside fails). These have different design implications — the first is a capacity problem, the second is a quality problem.

**Spontaneous collaboration (NODE-WO-03b):** This is the node most directly tied to the "things that can only happen in the office" value proposition. When spontaneous collaboration is happening effectively, it is a genuine office value-add that should be reported prominently. When it is absent — when the office design does not produce unplanned but productive interactions — this is a significant value proposition failure.

**Hybrid meeting two-tier analysis:** When technology enables full parity between in-room and remote participants, the hybrid meeting is a collaboration success. When technology creates a two-tier experience — in-room participants having better audio, video, and participation quality than remote ones — the hybrid meeting is a collaboration failure that also creates organizational equity issues. Report two-tier patterns explicitly.

## NODE-WO-04: Hybrid technology integration

**Technology failure versus technology design failure:** A one-time AV failure is a maintenance issue. A pattern of AV failures described across multiple sessions, or a consistently described pattern of employees defaulting to laptop cameras rather than room systems, is a technology design failure. Report the pattern, not the incident.

**The laptop-instead-of-room-system signal:** When employees describe using their personal laptops for video calls even when a room system is available, this is one of the most significant technology design findings in the domain. It indicates the room system is either unreliable, too complex, or produces inferior audio/video quality compared to the employee's personal setup. Report it with this interpretation.

## NODE-WO-05: Environmental comfort

**Report behavioral modifications alongside environmental findings.** "The open plan is noisy" is not a finding. "The open plan noise level consistently causes employees to move spaces, use headphones, or relocate to enclosed spaces to achieve any sustained concentration — with downstream effects on both productivity and desk utilization patterns" is a finding.

**Thermal complaints:** Temperature discomfort is systematically gendered in workplace research — women more frequently report being cold in air-conditioned office environments, men more frequently report being comfortable or too warm. When thermal findings are present, note whether they vary by respondent demographic if the sample allows.

## NODE-WO-06: Workplace identity and culture expression

**Do not use NODE-WO-06 positive findings to offset NODE-WO-02 or NODE-WO-04 negative findings.** An employee who is proud of how the office looks but cannot achieve focused work or reliable hybrid meeting technology has a workspace that is aesthetically successful and functionally inadequate. Report these separately.

## NODE-WO-07: Hybrid decision-making

**This is the node most directly connected to the client's attendance and design investment decisions.** Analyze it by extracting the specific factors the respondent named as tipping toward office attendance and toward home — and then connect each factor to a specific coverage node. A factor named as "when I have back-to-back meetings" connects to NODE-WO-03 (collaboration value). A factor named as "when I don't need to concentrate" connects to NODE-WO-02 (focus work inadequacy). The coverage node connection is what transforms a preference statement into a design recommendation.

**Office-wins versus home-wins analysis:** Categorize all named attendance factors as "office wins" (factors that tip toward coming in) or "home wins" (factors that tip toward remote). The relative weight of each category and the specific factors within it constitute the most direct evidence available about what the office currently offers and what it is failing to offer.

# Section 3 — Quality Weighting Rules

## Sessions with return-to-office tension

Apply across evaluative statements about the office: weight all global positive assessments of the office experience at 0.5 of face value. Weight all specific behavioral accounts and workaround descriptions at 1.0. Report: "This session showed evidence of return-to-office tension — the organizational context includes [management attendance priority / recent mandate discussion]. Global positive assessments have been treated conservatively. Specific behavioral accounts, workaround descriptions, and hybrid decision factors have been weighted as primary evidence."

## Evaluative statements versus behavioral evidence divergence

When a global evaluative statement ("the office is good for collaboration") diverges from the behavioral evidence (the respondent books meeting rooms for solo work, uses phone booths for focus sessions, and comes in only on meeting-heavy days), the behavioral evidence is the finding. Report: "The respondent's global assessment of the office was positive. However, the behavioral pattern described across the session — [specific workaround list] — suggests the office's value proposition is narrower than this assessment implies, with focus work support being the primary gap."

## Technology failure count weighting

When multiple technology failures are described in a single session, the cumulative effect on confidence in the hybrid model is greater than any individual incident. Each failure compounds. Report them cumulatively: "The respondent described [N] distinct technology failures across the session — [brief list]. The cumulative effect of this pattern on hybrid meeting confidence is significant and should be treated as a design finding rather than a series of isolated incidents."

# Section 4 — Benchmark Context Guide

## Valid comparisons for this domain

Benchmarks are valid when matched on: industry sector (different sectors show systematically different hybrid attendance norms and office usage patterns), office design typology (open-plan versus activity-based versus assigned-desk), and hybrid policy type (mandated days versus employee-discretion).

## Focus work satisfaction benchmarks

Must be segmented by office design type before citation. Open-plan offices show systematically lower focus work satisfaction than activity-based designs. Comparing an open-plan benchmark to an activity-based design, or vice versa, is invalid.

## Attendance benchmarks

Hybrid attendance benchmarks vary significantly by sector, role type, and whether the hybrid policy is mandated or discretionary. Always cite the matched benchmark — sector, role type, and policy type — before comparing.

## When not to cite benchmarks

Do not cite attendance benchmarks in sessions where return-to-office tension is flagged — the tension may be producing artificially high or low attendance that makes benchmark comparison misleading without additional context.

# Section 5 — Output Format Specification

## Executive summary

Must contain: the primary finding about the office's performance against its design intent. The workaround pattern summary if present. Data reliability note. Coverage completeness.

Example: "The central finding is a focus work gap — the office's open-plan design consistently fails to support the concentrated individual work that constitutes a significant portion of respondents' daily work mode, producing a pattern of phone booth substitution for solo focus sessions, meeting room booking for individual work, and selective attendance that favors meeting-heavy days over focus-work days. This finding directly addresses the attendance pattern decision map scenario and suggests that focus work space provision is the highest-priority design intervention. Data reliability was good; return-to-office tension was noted and has been accounted for in the weighting of evaluative statements."

## Findings sections

Five sections: Spatial Journey and Usage Patterns, Focus Work Support, Collaboration Space Effectiveness, Hybrid Technology Experience, Environmental Comfort. A sixth section — Office Value Proposition and Attendance Decision Factors — synthesizes the hybrid decision-making findings.

All physical findings must include their attendance implication. All technology findings must specify whether they indicate an incident or a pattern. All collaboration findings must distinguish planned from spontaneous collaboration quality.

## Recommendations section

One recommendation per decision map scenario. Each traceable to a specific finding and a specific design, technology, or spatial intervention. Do not recommend policy changes — this domain produces design recommendations, not policy recommendations. Do not recommend remediation for individual technology incidents — recommend systematic audit of all spaces showing the pattern.

# Section 6 — Multi-Session Analysis Guide

## Work mode segmentation

Segment by primary work mode: collaboration-primary employees (more than 60% of work time in meetings or group settings) and focus-primary employees (more than 60% of work time in individual work). These two profiles produce systematically different office experience profiles and must be analyzed separately before aggregate findings are reported.

## Workaround pattern analysis across sessions

Catalog all workarounds described across sessions and identify which are common (same workaround described by multiple respondents) versus individual. Common workarounds are the most important findings in multi-session analysis — they reveal systematic design failures rather than individual adaptations. "Booking meeting rooms for solo focus work was described independently by [N] of [total] respondents, all citing the same underlying cause: the open plan noise level exceeds their focus work threshold."

## Attendance factor distribution

Across multiple sessions, compile all office-wins and home-wins factors and report them as a ranked list by frequency of mention. This distribution is the most direct evidence available about what the office currently offers and what it is failing to offer at the aggregate level.

## Technology failure pattern identification

When the same technology failure — AV in a specific room type, connectivity in a specific area, a specific piece of equipment — appears across multiple sessions, classify it as a pattern rather than an incident. The pattern classification changes the recommendation from maintenance to design audit.

# Section 7 — Flagging and Limitation Language

## Focus work gap with attendance implications

"The focus work gap identified in this session — the respondent's description of [specific workarounds and their cause] — represents both a spatial design finding and an attendance driver. Until focus work space adequacy is addressed, this employee's attendance pattern will continue to favor meeting-heavy days, which limits the office's value proposition to collaborative use cases only."

## Return-to-office tension present

"This session was conducted in an organizational context where hybrid attendance has been a management priority. The respondent showed evidence of return-to-office tension — positive global assessments of the office that were not fully consistent with the behavioral pattern described. Specific behavioral accounts and hybrid decision factors have been weighted above evaluative statements throughout this report."

## Single-session limitation for design recommendations

"Design intervention recommendations based on a single session should be treated as directional hypotheses rather than validated findings. A minimum of [five sessions for focus work and hybrid findings / eight sessions for comprehensive design audit recommendations] is required before investing in physical redesign. The current finding identifies the priority investigation area, not the confirmed design solution."

## Technology pattern versus incident limitation

"The technology failures described in this session may represent isolated incidents or may be part of a systematic pattern. Without [N] additional sessions confirming the same failure type, the finding should be treated as a flag for investigation rather than a confirmed design failure. A cross-session analysis following [minimum session count] will determine whether these observations constitute a reportable pattern."
