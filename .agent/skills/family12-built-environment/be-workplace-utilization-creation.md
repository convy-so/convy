# Section 1 — Domain Identity

Workplace and Office Environment research is the systematic investigation of how the physical workspace affects the people who work in it — their productivity, their collaboration quality, their focus capability, their sense of professional identity, and their experience of the hybrid working model. It is distinct from Employee Engagement research, which studies the psychological relationship between an employee and their organization. This domain is specifically concerned with the built environment as a medium for work: how the physical design, spatial configuration, environmental conditions, and technology infrastructure of a workspace support or obstruct different types of work.

The domain is particularly focused on the hybrid dimension — the relationship between the physical office and the digital workspace in organizations where employees split their time between on-site and remote work. Hybrid working has fundamentally changed the purpose and value proposition of the office: it is no longer a place you come to because you have to, but a place you choose to come to because it offers something the home environment cannot. Understanding what that something is, or why it is absent, is the central research question of this domain.

The domain covers four distinct dimensions. Physical comfort and environmental quality — the thermal, acoustic, lighting, and ergonomic conditions that determine whether the space is physically comfortable to inhabit for extended periods. Spatial design for work modes — whether the range of spaces available (focused work zones, collaborative spaces, informal social areas, quiet rooms) matches the range of work modes employees actually need to support. Technology and connectivity infrastructure — whether the hardware, audiovisual systems, and digital connectivity tools work reliably and support seamless hybrid collaboration. Identity and culture expression — whether the physical environment reflects and reinforces the organization's stated culture and gives employees a sense of professional pride in their workplace.

This domain is not appropriate for studying employee engagement, job satisfaction, or organizational culture in the abstract — that belongs to Employee Engagement and Culture Assessment research. It is not appropriate for evaluating remote working policy or the fairness of hybrid working arrangements — those are organizational design questions. It is not appropriate for studying neighborhood or transit experience — that belongs to Community and Neighborhood Research.

# Section 2 — Research Questions This Domain Can and Cannot Answer

## Questions this domain is equipped to answer

- How does the physical office environment support or hinder the different types of work employees need to do — focused individual work, collaborative team work, informal relationship-building, and concentrated creative work?
- What is the quality of the employee's experience of the hybrid model — does the physical office complement the remote work environment or create friction with it?
- Which specific physical features, zones, or infrastructure elements most strongly drive the decision to come into the office versus work remotely?
- What environmental conditions — acoustic, thermal, lighting — most frequently interrupt or prevent productive work in the office?
- How does the physical workspace reflect and reinforce (or contradict) the organization's culture, and what is the employee's sense of professional pride in their physical work environment?
- What is the employee's observed journey through the office during a typical day — which spaces they use, in what sequence, and why — and what does that reveal about spatial utilization and design effectiveness?

## Questions this domain cannot answer

- Whether an employee is satisfied with their role, their manager, or their organization — redirect to Employee Engagement research
- Whether the hybrid working policy is fair or well-designed — that is an organizational design question
- Whether the organization's culture is healthy or aligned with its values — redirect to Culture Assessment research
- Whether a specific space or building should be purchased, leased, or vacated — that is a real estate decision requiring financial analysis, not qualitative research

# Section 3 — Brief Interrogation Guide

## 3.1 Office purpose and hybrid model

**Information required:** The stated purpose of the office in this organization's hybrid model — whether it is primarily for collaboration, focus work, client-facing activity, culture-building, or some combination — and the current hybrid working pattern (how many days per week employees are expected or choose to come in).

**Complete answer:** "Our official policy is two days per week in the office, with no mandated specific days. We designed the office for collaboration — we have no assigned desks, predominantly open collaboration spaces, and limited quiet zones. But we're finding attendance is lower than expected and employees are clustering on Tuesday and Thursday which is creating a density problem."

**Incomplete answer:** "It's a hybrid office." The stated purpose and the actual usage pattern are both required — and when they diverge, that divergence is itself a central research finding.

**Follow-up probe:** "When you designed the office — or when it was last significantly changed — what was the primary use case you were designing for? And how closely does the observed usage pattern match that intent?"

**Required or optional:** Required.

## 3.2 Space inventory and design intent

**Information required:** A description of the types of spaces available — open plan, quiet zones, private meeting rooms, phone booths, informal social areas, outdoor spaces — and in what proportion. The research brief need not be architecturally precise but must be sufficient for the Conducting agent to ask informed questions about specific space types.

**Complete answer:** "We have 400 desks in an open plan, 20 private meeting rooms ranging from 4 to 20 people, 15 phone booths, a café area, and a rooftop terrace. We have no designated quiet zones and no permanent assigned desks."

**Follow-up probe:** "Are there space types that employees have asked for that don't exist? And are there space types that exist but seem consistently under-used based on booking data or observation?"

**Required or optional:** Required.

## 3.3 Technology and hybrid infrastructure

**Information required:** The primary technology tools employees use to bridge physical and digital working — video conferencing systems, booking apps, collaboration platforms — and any known technology friction points.

**Complete answer:** "We use Teams for all digital collaboration. Every meeting room has a Teams Room setup. We have a desk booking app that about 60% of employees use. Our main technology complaints in the last survey were about meeting room AV quality and connectivity on the roof terrace."

**Required or optional:** Required. Technology infrastructure is a critical node in hybrid workplace research and the Conducting agent must know what exists in order to ask about it intelligently.

## 3.4 Attendance patterns and design tension

**Information required:** What the current attendance pattern looks like versus what was expected or desired, and whether there is a specific tension between the office design and how employees actually use it.

**Complete answer:** "Attendance runs about 55% of the expected level. People cluster on certain days which creates overcrowding while the space sits empty on others. We also see employees using meeting rooms for solo focus work because we don't have enough quiet options — which blocks rooms for actual meetings."

**Follow-up probe:** "Is there a specific frustration or workaround behavior you see employees using — booking meeting rooms for solo work, working from a coffee shop instead of the office, clustering in certain areas and avoiding others — that you want this research to explain?"

**Required or optional:** Required. Known workaround behaviors are among the most valuable research inputs in this domain.

## 3.5 Organizational context

**Information required:** What has changed recently in the organization — headcount growth or reduction, office relocation, hybrid policy changes, return-to-office mandates — that might be shaping employee attitudes to the physical workspace.

**Complete answer:** "We moved to this building eighteen months ago from an older, assigned-desk model. The move to hot-desking and open plan was controversial at the time and there is still residual negative sentiment among a subset of the workforce, particularly longer-tenured employees."

**Required or optional:** Advisory but important. Historical context shapes how employees describe current experience.

# Section 4 — Decision Map Interrogation

## Interrogation framework

**For positive findings:**
"If the research shows that the physical environment is supporting the work modes employees need — that people feel the office adds something their home environment doesn't — what specifically changes? Is there a real estate decision, a design investment, or a policy change this would support?"

**For negative findings:**
"If the research identifies specific spatial or infrastructure failures that are driving employees to use the office less than desired — what is the realistic intervention? Is this a design change, a technology upgrade, a policy adjustment, or a cultural shift?"

**For mixed findings:**
"If collaboration spaces work well but focus work is inadequately supported — or technology works everywhere except the spaces where it's most needed — how do you prioritize the design response?"

## Complete decision map example

Positive: "If the research shows employees value the collaborative energy of the office and use it for the purpose we designed it for, we will use the findings to strengthen the case for our hybrid model at the upcoming board review."

Negative: "If the research identifies specific spaces or infrastructure elements that are reliably deterring office attendance, we will commission a design audit of those specific elements within two months of receiving findings."

Mixed: "If focus work is the under-served use case — employees want to come in but can't get the quiet they need — we will convert two under-booked meeting rooms into bookable quiet pods before the next quarter."

# Section 6 — Audience Model Interrogation Guide

## Psychographic dimensions to capture

**Work mode profile:** Whether the employee's work is primarily individual and focused, primarily collaborative and meeting-heavy, or mixed. This determines which nodes are most relevant and which spatial features matter most to this particular respondent.

**Hybrid attitude:** Whether the employee is a willing hybrid worker, a reluctant office attendee, or a preference-for-office worker. These profiles produce very different research findings on identical physical environments.

**Tenure and office memory:** Employees who joined during remote-only periods have no pre-hybrid office baseline to compare against. Longer-tenured employees compare the current office against a remembered previous experience. These frames produce very different evaluation contexts.

## Questions to surface these dimensions

"On a week when you're in the office the expected number of days — what kind of work do you typically come in to do? Is it mostly meetings and collaboration, mostly individual work, or a mix?"

"If you could choose entirely freely — no policy expectations at all — how often would you come into the office? And why that number?"

## Known sensitivity flags

Return-to-office tension: in organizations where hybrid policy has been controversial or where office attendance has been lower than management desired, employees may be cautious about describing the office as inadequate — they may perceive this as supporting a case for further mandated attendance. Conversely, they may overstate problems if they are trying to resist attendance requirements. The agent must be aware of this dynamic and build explicit neutrality before approaching office attendance questions.

# Section 7 — Constitutional Constraints

## Blocking constraints

**Constraint B-1: The office design intent must be clearly stated.** A research brief that cannot articulate what the office was designed for cannot produce a gap analysis between intent and experience. The agent must probe until the design intent is clearly stated — whether collaboration-primary, focus-primary, or hybrid-purpose.

**Constraint B-2: The decision map must include at least one physically actionable intervention.** A brief that produces only policy recommendations (change the hybrid working policy) without any design-level recommendation is incomplete — this domain is concerned with the physical environment.

## Advisory constraints

**Advisory A-1: If return-to-office tension exists, record in expertState.** This tension shapes how employees describe the office and must be noted for the Analytics agent.

**Advisory A-2: If the office has recently relocated or been redesigned, record the timeline.** Employees who experienced the previous environment hold a comparison baseline that shapes their evaluation of the current one.
