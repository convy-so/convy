## Section 1: What This Domain's Data Actually Means

Client Relationship Research data is about trust and perceived value in a specific professional relationship — not about transactional satisfaction, not about brand sentiment, but about the quality of a relationship that has a named account structure, a history, and a future that both parties are invested in. The Analytics agent's central interpretive task is to separate what the data says about the person on the provider side from what it says about the institution — because these two assessments carry fundamentally different action implications.

**The person-institution divergence is the primary structural finding:** When the session documents a client whose relationship health is significantly better than or worse than their assessment of the institution, this divergence is the primary finding. A strong account manager relationship with weak institutional backing is a person-dependence risk — the relationship is one personnel change away from becoming vulnerable. A weak account manager relationship with strong institutional confidence suggests an account assignment problem rather than an institutional failure. The Analytics agent must report these divergences explicitly, not average them.

**What proactivity evidence means:** Proactivity — the account manager bringing value to the client before being asked — is the single strongest differentiator between professional service relationships that generate loyalty and those that generate only adequacy. When the session documents proactive behavior with specific instances, this is the relationship's primary health signal. When it documents a reactive relationship — the provider responds well but does not initiate — this is a vulnerability regardless of the respondent's satisfaction level, because a reactive relationship is highly substitutable by any equally competent competitor.

**What the absence test reveals:** When the Conducting agent used the absence test ("what would you miss if the relationship ended?") and the respondent could name specific things, these named elements are the relationship's genuine value anchors. When the respondent could not name specific things despite overall positive sentiment, the relationship's value is diffuse and therefore fragile. Diffuse value is difficult to defend against a specific competitive offer.

**What the leverage dynamic looks like:** When the session shows an unusually high proportion of negative findings with unusual specificity about commercial terms — pricing, scope, value for money — and the brief documented an active negotiation, the Analytics agent must flag this as a potentially strategy-weighted session. The findings may still be genuine, but they should be reported with the caveat that the respondent may have shaped their emphasis toward commercially useful concerns.

**What managed satisfaction means:** When the session documents uniformly professional-positive assessments with no emotional content, no specific evidence, no vulnerability signals, and no expansion aspirations — a relationship that is described entirely in professional adequacy language — this is the managed satisfaction profile. This is not a positive finding. It is a relationship that exists because of switching cost or inertia rather than genuine preference. It is highly vulnerable to a well-timed competitive approach.

## Section 2: Coverage Interpretation Guide

**Overall Health — Positive with Declining Direction:**
A relationship that is currently positive but trending downward is more urgent than a relationship that is currently negative but stable. The direction signal is often more actionable than the current position. Report the direction explicitly: "The relationship is currently [characterization] but the session indicates a declining trajectory. The primary drivers of this decline are [identified factors]."

**Account Manager Quality — High, Institutional Quality — Low:**
Person-dependence risk finding. The relationship is contingent on the named individual. The vulnerability must be reported explicitly: "The relationship health is primarily attributable to [account manager name]'s performance. The institutional quality — assessed independently of [name] — was characterized as [characterization]. This profile represents a significant succession risk."

**Account Manager Quality — Low, Institutional Quality — High:**
Account assignment problem. The institution is performing well but the named account manager is not the right fit for this client. This is an addressable finding — a personnel change could improve the relationship without institutional change.

**Proactivity — Low, Competence — High:**
Reactive relationship. Technically adequate but strategically substitutable. Report: "The account management relationship is characterized as responsive rather than proactive. While technical delivery quality is positively assessed, the absence of proactive value-creation means the relationship's competitive differentiation is limited to execution capability rather than strategic insight."

**Value Perception — Diffuse (no specific anchors):**
Fragile value. "Value is assessed positively but the respondent could not identify specific anchors for that value when asked what they would miss if the relationship ended. Diffuse value perception is a vulnerability — it is difficult to defend against a specific competitive offer and may not withstand a pricing or scope change."

**Person-Dependence — Confirmed with High Switching Signal:**
Highest-priority risk finding in this domain. "The relationship is person-dependent and the respondent indicated moderate-to-high switching consideration if [name] were to leave. This represents a specific, identifiable risk event — any change in account management assignment should be managed as a relationship retention intervention."

## Section 3: Quality Weighting Rules

**Sessions with leverage dynamic signals:**
Weight up the negative findings (they are likely genuine) and weight down the unusual specificity of commercial concerns (these may be negotiation-motivated rather than analytically representative). Include the findings with the caveat that the active negotiation context may have shaped the emphasis of the session.

**Sessions with loyalty-to-individual confound:**
When the respondent showed clear loyalty to the account manager that was moderating their institutional critique, use the specific institutional quality evidence from the session over the general positive relationship framing. The general positive framing reflects the loyalty; the specific institutional evidence is the analytical content.

**Sessions with managed satisfaction profile:**
Do not report as positive findings. The managed satisfaction profile is a risk signal, not a health signal. Report it as: "The relationship appears adequate but lacks emotional investment or specific value anchors. This profile is characteristic of a relationship maintained by switching cost rather than genuine preference and should be treated as a retention risk."

**Sessions where the person-institution separation was not achieved:**
If the Conducting agent flagged that the separation could not be established — the respondent blended their assessments of the individual and the institution throughout — note this explicitly and report the blended findings with the caveat that person-level and institution-level attribution cannot be separated from this session.

## Section 4: Benchmark Context

**Professional service relationship benchmarks by sector:** Management consulting, advertising agency, legal services, financial advisory, and enterprise software vendor relationships have different structural norms for account management intensity, proactivity expectations, and relationship tenure benchmarks. Always use sector-appropriate benchmarks.

**Account manager tenure benchmarks:** The relationship quality impact of account manager tenure is well-documented and sector-specific. A relationship below the sector average tenure for effective account management should be flagged as a structural risk regardless of current quality scores.

**Person-dependence risk prevalence:** Benchmarks for person-dependence risk in professional service relationships provide context for whether a specific finding is unusual or sector-typical. When person-dependence is typical for the sector, the finding is a risk flag but not an unusual one; when it is below sector norms, it is a more significant finding.

**What benchmarks cannot tell the client:** A relationship at benchmark does not mean it is robust. In most professional service sectors, benchmark performance includes a significant proportion of relationships that are managed-satisfaction profiles — adequate but not genuinely loyal. Benchmark parity should not be presented as a health signal without this context.

## Section 5: Output Format Specification

**Section 1: Study Parameters**
Relationship context: provider type, client profile, relationship tenure, account manager tenure, scope of studied relationship, any recent relationship changes. Factual only.

**Section 2: Executive Summary**
Two to four sentences. Primary finding: relationship health characterization, direction of travel, and the primary risk or strength factor. Person-institution divergence if present. Connection to the decision map's relevant outcome.

**Section 3: Overall Relationship Health**
Current state, direction of travel, and emotional quality of the partnership. Distinguish between professionally positive and emotionally invested. Flag managed satisfaction or declining trajectory where present.

**Section 4: Account Manager Assessment**
Separate analysis of personal trust and chemistry versus proactivity and business understanding. Both must be addressed independently. Note the proactivity finding explicitly — reactive vs. proactive is a primary risk or strength indicator.

**Section 5: Institutional Assessment**
The institution's delivery quality, assessed independently of the account manager. Note any divergence from the account manager assessment. Person-dependence risk finding goes here, based on the person-institution comparison.

**Section 6: Value Analysis**
Value perception, with specific anchors where identified. Absence test findings where used. Comparison signal if present. Characterize value as concrete (specific anchors) or diffuse (general positive without anchors).

**Section 7: Relationship Resilience Assessment**
Person-dependence risk: explicitly assessed against the scenario probe. Expansion potential: unaddressed needs or scope growth indicators. Renewal intent and switching trigger findings.

**Section 8: Decision Map Response**
Map each finding to its decision map entry. Address every entry.

**Section 9: Limitations and Confidence Notes**
Required. Use the specific language from Section 7.

## Section 6: Multi-Session Analysis Guide

**Pattern vs. account-specific finding:**
In Client Relationship Research, findings from a single session about a single relationship are account-specific. Patterns across multiple sessions with different client contacts at the same organization, or across multiple clients of the same provider, are structural findings about the provider's relationship management capability.

**Person-dependence prevalence:**
When multiple sessions across different client accounts all show person-dependence, this is a finding about the provider's institutional relationship management capability — not a series of individual relationship profiles. It indicates that the provider's value delivery is systematically concentrated in its account managers rather than distributed through the institutional model.

**Proactivity patterns:**
If multiple client sessions document reactive relationships with a single provider, this is an account management culture finding — the provider has not built proactivity into its account management model. This is a structural finding about how the provider manages its clients, not a series of individual account manager assessments.

## Section 7: Flagging and Limitation Language

**When person-dependence risk is confirmed:**
"The session confirms a person-dependent relationship: the health of the partnership is primarily attributable to [name]'s performance, and the respondent indicated [characterization of switching risk] if [name] were to leave. This is the primary risk finding of this session. The recommended action is [account succession planning / institutional depth-building / relationship expansion beyond the named contact], to be addressed before any account management change occurs."

**When managed satisfaction profile is present:**
"The session presents a managed satisfaction profile: the relationship is assessed as adequate in all dimensions but shows no emotional investment, no specific value anchors, and no expansion aspirations. This profile is characteristic of a relationship maintained by switching cost rather than genuine preference. It should be treated as a retention risk, particularly in the event of a pricing change, a competitive approach, or an account manager change."

**When leverage dynamic is present:**
"The session was conducted during an active contract negotiation, which the Conducting agent identified as a potential source of strategic emphasis in the respondent's account. The negative findings — particularly on [commercial dimensions] — may reflect genuine concerns or may reflect negotiation-motivated emphasis. Findings on these dimensions should be corroborated through a session conducted outside the negotiation context before being treated as representative of the ongoing relationship quality."

**When the person-institution separation was not achieved:**
"The person-institution separation — the independent assessment of [name]'s contribution versus [company]'s institutional contribution — could not be achieved in this session. The respondent's assessments blended the two throughout. Findings on account manager quality and institutional quality cannot be attributed independently and should be reported as blended relationship assessments."

**When session count is insufficient for relationship pattern claims:**
"Findings from this session are account-specific and describe one client contact's assessment of one relationship. They cannot support claims about the provider's relationship management capability across its client base without corroborating sessions from other account relationships."
